\section{General context}
\label{sec1:general}

\newglossaryentry{AECO}{name={AECO}, description={Architecture, Engineering, Construction and Operations}}
\newglossaryentry{BIM}{name={BIM}, description={Building Information Model}}
\newglossaryentry{DRM}{name={DRM}, description={Dynamic Reality Model}}
\newglossaryentry{XR}{name={XR}, description={Extended Reality}}

%  introduce industry context and motivation for digital transformation
The Architecture, Engineering, Construction and Operations (\gls{AECO}) industry is undergoing a rapid digital transformation, driven by advances in reality capture technologies such as laser scanning and photogrammetry~\cite{bassier_geomapi_2024}. These technologies enable the efficient acquisition of high-resolution 3D models of the physical environment, forming a foundation for data-driven workflows and digital twins~\cite{coupry_bim-based_2021}.

%  introduce the two dominant modelling approaches (scan vs BIM)
Current digital representations of the built environment are primarily derived from two distinct modelling approaches. Reality capture-based models provide highly detailed and visually accurate reconstructions of real-world scenes, making them well suited for documentation, inspection and visualization~\cite{bassier_standalone_2016}. In contrast, Building Information Modelling (\gls{BIM}) represents environments as structured collections of semantic, parametric objects, enabling efficient querying, modification and simulation~\cite{bassier_processing_2020}.

%  compare strengths and limitations of scan vs BIM
Each approach offers distinct advantages, but also exhibits inherent limitations. Reality capture models preserve geometric and visual fidelity, yet remain static, incomplete and unstructured, lacking explicit object decomposition and semantic relationships~\cite{geyter_point_2022}. In contrast, BIM models provide a dynamic and structured representation of buildings, but often lack geometric detail and visual realism, as they are typically created during the design phase and do not fully reflect as-built conditions~\cite{bassier_processing_2020}. (Figure \ref{fig:1_Scan_VS_BIM})

  \centering

  \caption[Scan vs. BIM]{Comparison between reality capture and Building Information Modelling approaches.}
  \label{fig:1_Scan_VS_BIM}
\end{figure}

%  identify core limitation of existing approaches and motivate need for new representation
This contrast highlights a fundamental limitation in current digital modelling workflows: no single representation simultaneously provides realism, completeness and interactivity.

%  introduce DRM as the central concept and contribution
To address this limitation, this research introduces a novel representation, referred to as a \textit{Dynamic Reality Model} (\gls{DRM}). The \gls{DRM} represents the environment as a collection of individual, manipulable objects, while preserving the geometric and visual fidelity of the captured scene. In doing so, it combines the structured and dynamic nature of BIM with the highly accurate and visually detailed reality capture data in a unified representation that supports both analysis and interaction.

%  provide concrete applications and justify relevance
DRMs enable a range of applications that require both accuracy and flexibility. In renovation workflows, designers can evaluate design alternatives directly within the as-is environment, reducing the need for manual reconstruction~\cite{vermandere_two-step_2022}. In facility management, objects such as furniture or equipment can be repositioned or replaced while maintaining consistency with real-world constraints~\cite{coupry_bim-based_2021}. Furthermore, the combination of visual fidelity and object-level interactivity enables the use of real-world environments in simulation, training and gaming applications~\cite{alizadehsalehi_bim_2020}.

%  transition to core challenges preventing DRM realization
Despite this potential, two fundamental barriers prevent \gls{DRM}s from being efficiently and reliably produced from existing building data.

%  explain first barrier — incompleteness and lack of structure
The first fundamental barrier is the intrinsic incompleteness and unstructured nature of captured data. Scan-based devices record only surfaces visible from their measurement positions, leaving significant portions of every scene unobserved due to occlusions~\cite{vermandere_guided_2025}. Even dense point clouds therefore contain gaps at multiple scales. In addition, captured data lacks explicit structural organisation, representing scenes as undifferentiated collections of points or surfaces without object-level decomposition~\cite{geyter_point_2022}. This makes the extraction of manipulable entities a non-trivial task.

%  explain second barrier — temporal staleness and updating difficulty
The second fundamental barrier is temporal staleness. Buildings are dynamic environments: furniture is rearranged, equipment is replaced, and layouts evolve over time~\cite{vermandere_two-step_2022}. Any captured dataset therefore begins to diverge from reality immediately after acquisition. While high-end scanning provides accurate data, frequent full re-capture is impractical~\cite{bassier_standalone_2016}. More accessible mobile and \gls{XR}-based devices introduce additional heterogeneity in accuracy and coverage~\cite{vermandere_automatic_2022}. Maintaining an up-to-date representation therefore requires incremental integration of heterogeneous, multi-temporal observations.

%  highlight interaction between both barriers and reinforce difficulty
These two barriers reinforce each other: incomplete data makes updating harder, while changes over time worsen inconsistencies in partial observations. Together, they explain why achieving visual quality, complete geometry and object-level interactivity at the same time remains difficult.

%  review existing work categories
Existing research has made significant progress in addressing individual aspects of this problem. Scene-level completion methods reconstruct missing geometry from partial observations~\cite{dai_scancomplete_2018, dai_spsg_2020, roldao_3d_2022, hubner_voxel-based_2020}, while object-level approaches focus on reconstructing individual objects using learned representations~\cite{mittal_autosdf_2022, chibane_implicit_2020, ren_xcube_2023, cheng_sdfusion_2023}. Other methods improve visual quality through texture reconstruction and clutter removal~\cite{suvorov_resolution-robust_2022, wei_clutter_2023, slavcheva_empty_2024, dolhasz_defurnishing_2025}, and some approaches attempt scene decomposition~\cite{li_hybrid_2024, ardelean_gen3dsr_2025}. 

%  critique limitations of existing work and position research gap
However, these approaches remain fundamentally limited in scope. They typically operate on static datasets, do not address multi-temporal integration, and do not support persistent object-level interaction. As a result, no unified framework currently exists that can transform partial, heterogeneous scan data into complete, structured and dynamically editable representations.

\section{Problem statement}
\label{sec2:problem}

%  define overall problem as multi-stage pipeline challenge
Transforming partially scanned indoor environments into complete, structured and dynamic representations remains an open challenge in current 3D reconstruction workflows. This challenge is inherently multi-stage, requiring the integration of heterogeneous data, the recovery of missing geometry and the transformation of static reconstructions into interactive environments.

%  define alignment and updating challenge
A first challenge lies in the alignment and updating of heterogeneous data. Different sensing technologies produce data with varying levels of accuracy, density and noise, resulting in inconsistent representations of the same environment~\cite{geyter_point_2022}. Aligning these data sources into a common reference frame is particularly difficult in indoor settings, where global positioning systems are unavailable~\cite{vermandere_two-step_2022}. In addition, environments evolve over time, requiring new partial observations to be integrated incrementally without reprocessing the entire dataset~\cite{vermandere_automatic_2022}.

%  define incompleteness challenge (referencing earlier section)
A second major challenge is the incompleteness of reality capture data, as introduced in Section~\ref{sec1:general}. Occlusions lead to missing geometry at both scene and object level~\cite{vermandere_synthetic_2026, vermandere_geometry_2025}. Effective completion requires not only recovering missing geometry, but also reliably separating individual objects in cluttered environments where boundaries are ambiguous~\cite{vermandere_guided_2025}.

%  highlight scene vs object completion gap
A deeper difficulty is that scene-level and object-level completion are typically treated as separate problems. Scene-level methods reconstruct geometry globally but cannot resolve occlusions between contacting objects. Object-level methods require pre-segmented objects and discard contextual information necessary for plausible reconstruction. Resolving this mutual dependency requires a framework in which scene and object completion are jointly addressed through shared constraints.

%  define dynamification challenge
Even when complete reconstructions are available, transforming them into dynamic and interactive environments remains non-trivial. This requires structuring geometry into representations that support object-level manipulation and interaction~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025,vermandere_adaptive_2026}.

%  define evaluation challenge
A final challenge lies in evaluation. Ground truth for occluded regions is rarely available in real-world datasets~\cite{dai_scannet_2017, chang_matterport3d_2017}. Synthetic datasets offer an alternative, but must accurately reproduce real-world scanning characteristics to ensure valid evaluation~\cite{de_geyter_automated_2022,vermandere_synthetic_2026}.

%  explicitly state central research gap
The absence of a unified framework that jointly addresses alignment, completion, and dynamification across heterogeneous and multi-temporal data constitutes the central research gap addressed in this thesis.

\section{Research aim}
\label{sec3:researchaim}

%  define main objective
The primary objective of this research is to develop a modular and adaptable methodology for transforming partially scanned indoor environments into complete, structured and dynamic representations, referred to as \gls{DRM}s.

%  justify modular pipeline design
Rather than relying on a monolithic end-to-end solution, the proposed methodology adopts a modular design. This is essential to handle the heterogeneity, incompleteness and temporal variability of real-world scan data, where end-to-end approaches lack robustness and interpretability. Each module performs a specific task and produces intermediate outputs that can be validated and refined.

The pipeline consists of the following stages, as outlined in Figure \ref{fig:1_Research_Aim}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scene alignment and updating}
    \item \textbf{Scene segmentation and object isolation}
    \item \textbf{Scene completion}
    \item \textbf{Object completion}
    \item \textbf{Scene dynamification}
\end{itemize}

  \centering

  \caption[Research aim pipeline]{Overview of the proposed modular pipeline.}
  \label{fig:1_Research_Aim}
\end{figure}

%  highlight key conceptual contribution
A key contribution of this work is the introduction of the \gls{DRM} as a representation that enables persistent object-level interaction while preserving scan-level fidelity.

To support evaluation, this research introduces a synthetic dataset generation framework that produces partial scans paired with complete ground-truth geometry. Because real-world datasets cannot provide ground truth for occluded regions, the very regions that completion methods must reconstruct, such a synthetic dataset is essential for quantitative assessment under controlled conditions that reflect real-world scanning characteristics.

The scope of this research is limited to single-room indoor environments containing furniture or industrial objects. This constraint enables a focused investigation of densely furnished scenes, where occlusions, object interactions and data heterogeneity are significant but manageable.

In summary, this research establishes a modular pipeline that integrates alignment, segmentation, completion and dynamification into a coherent workflow. By combining the strengths of reality capture and structured representations, the proposed methodology enables the creation of dynamic models from partial scan data.

\section{Thesis Outline}

%  explain structure of thesis
This thesis follows the transformation from raw scan data to dynamic representations.

Chapter \ref{ch:background} introduces the background and terminology related to scene dynamification and 3D representations. It defines key concepts such as dynamic scenes, object-level representations and completion, positions the proposed \gls{DRM} within the broader context of existing modelling paradigms, and explains the conceptual role of each pipeline stage.

Chapter \ref{ch:3_Data_Acquisition} focuses on data acquisition, covering both real-world scanning and synthetic dataset generation. It discusses the characteristics and limitations of different sensing technologies, including occlusions, noise and coverage, and introduces the V-Scan synthetic dataset designed to enable controlled evaluation of reconstruction methods.

Chapter \ref{ch:4_Alignment_Updating} addresses the alignment and updating of multi-source and multi-temporal data. It presents methods for integrating heterogeneous datasets into a consistent representation and explores strategies for incrementally updating scenes as new observations become available.

Chapter \ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation} focuses on object detection and occlusion analysis. It introduces methods for decomposing unstructured scan data into individual objects and for estimating occluded regions at both object and scene level, providing the basis for subsequent completion steps.

Chapter \ref{ch:6_Scene_Completion} presents the scene completion methodology, focusing on the reconstruction of structural elements of the environment. It describes how incomplete observations are converted into a consistent geometric and textured representation of the scene.

Chapter \ref{ch:7_Object_Completion} explores object completion techniques, comparing different approaches for reconstructing partially observed objects. The chapter evaluates their performance and discusses their integration within the overall pipeline.

Chapter \ref{ch:8_Scene_Dynamification} introduces the dynamification of completed scenes. It describes how static reconstructions are transformed into structured, interactive representations, enabling object-level manipulation and interaction within the environment.

Chapter \ref{ch:conclusion} concludes the thesis by summarizing the main contributions and discussing the limitations of the proposed methodology, as well as directions for future research.

Chapter \ref{ch:Valorisation} explores the valorization potential of the research, highlighting applications in areas such as renovation, facility management and interactive environments, and discussing opportunities for real-world deployment.

Through this structured approach, the thesis reflects the full transformation pipeline from partial scan data to dynamic and interactive scene representations.
