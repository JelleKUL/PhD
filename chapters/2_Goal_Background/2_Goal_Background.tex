\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:bg_intro}

% remind what a DRM is and introduce the term "scene dynamification"
The previous chapter introduced the \gls{DRM} as a representation that combines the geometric and visual fidelity of reality capture with the structured, interactive nature of object-based models. In this thesis, we define \textit{scene dynamification} as the process of producing said \gls{DRM}: transforming a static, unstructured 3D reconstruction into a structured collection of individually manipulable objects embedded within a geometrically and visually complete environment.

% explain the outline of the chapter
This chapter establishes the conceptual and terminological foundation for the remainder of the thesis by defining the scope and requirements of the dynamification process and positioning it relative to existing representations. Section~\ref{sec:bg_representations} reviews the landscape of 3D representations and discusses their suitability as inputs and outputs of the proposed pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:bg_drm_requirements} formalises the requirements of a \gls{DRM}, including its inputs, outputs, structural properties, and the occlusion categories the pipeline must address. Section~\ref{sec:bg_pipeline_overview} presents the conceptual pipeline and motivates the parallel treatment of scene and object completion. Section~\ref{sec:bg_sota_dynamification} surveys existing work most directly related to scene dynamification as a unified problem. Section~\ref{sec:bg_conclusion} concludes with a summary of the scope and assumptions adopted throughout the thesis.

\section{3D Scene Representations}
\label{sec:bg_representations}

A clear understanding of scene dynamification requires an overview of the main ways in which 3D geometry and appearance can be represented digitally. The chosen representation strongly influences which operations are possible, how missing geometry can be reconstructed, how colour can be stored, and how easily a scene can be edited or interacted with afterwards. In general, 3D representations can be divided into two broad categories: \textit{explicit} and \textit{implicit} representations.

Explicit representations store geometry directly as points, surfaces or volume elements. They are often intuitive to understand and well suited for visualization and geometric processing. Implicit representations describe geometry indirectly through mathematical functions defined over space. They are especially useful in reconstruction, completion and learning-based methods. Each type has advantages and limitations depending on the intended application.

% ── Explicit ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Explicit Representations}
\label{sec:bg_explicit}

Explicit representations can be visualised directly in any 3D software, without the need for any extra processing. This makes them practical for rendering, editing and many downstream applications (Figure~\ref{fig:2_Explicit_Reps}).

\paragraph{Point clouds.}

A point cloud is a collection of sampled 3D points,
\[
P = \{\mathbf{x}_i\}_{i=1}^{N},
\]
where each point $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{3}$ represents a measured position on a surface. Additional information such as colour or surface normals can also be stored per point.

Point clouds are the natural output of laser scanners, depth cameras and mobile sensing devices, and form the main raw input used throughout this thesis. Their main advantage is that they closely reflect real physical measurements. They are simple to acquire, scalable to large scenes and can capture fine geometric detail when densely sampled.

However, point clouds do not contain surface connectivity. In other words, neighbouring points are not explicitly linked to form surfaces. This makes tasks such as rendering, mesh generation or watertight reconstruction more difficult. Their quality also depends strongly on the sensing device used, ranging from highly accurate terrestrial laser scanners to noisier mobile and \gls{XR}-based systems~\cite{bassier_standalone_2016, vermandere_two-step_2022, de_geyter_automated_2022}.

\paragraph{Meshes.}

A mesh represents a surface using connected vertices, edges and faces. In practice, most meshes consist of triangles, making them efficient to process and render. A mesh can be written as
\[
M = (V,E,F),
\]
where $V$ is the set of vertices, $E$ the edges and $F$ the faces.

Meshes are the standard representation in computer graphics because they explicitly define surfaces and support efficient rendering. They are also widely used for simulation, collision detection and geometric editing.

Colour can be stored per vertex or face, but is often stored separately as a texture image. Each point on the mesh surface is linked to a location in a 2D image through \gls{UV} coordinates~\cite{heckbert_survey_1986}. This allows the 3d object to appear more detailed and realistic, without having to add more geometric complexity. However, this also introduces extra complexity when the mesh needs to be repaired or completed~\cite{vermandere_semantic_2024}.

Mesh representations from indoor scans are often reconstructed using a meshing algorithm, since this process in not perfect, the results contain defects such as holes, incorrect connections or non-manifold geometry. This means that extra repair steps are commonly required before further use~\cite{geyter_point_2022, attene_lightweight_2010}.

\paragraph{Voxel grids.}

Voxel grids divide 3D space into small cubic cells, called voxels, similar to how pixels divide a 2D image. A voxel grid can be written as
\[
V = \{v_{ijk}\},
\]
where each voxel stores information such as occupancy, colour or semantic labels.

Instead of representing only observed surfaces, voxel grids represent the full volume of space. This makes them useful for reasoning about empty and occupied regions, collision detection, visibility analysis and shape completion. Because they are arranged on a regular grid, they are also well suited for 3D convolutional neural networks and are widely used in scene completion research~\cite{dai_scancomplete_2018, dai_spsg_2020}.

Their main disadvantage is memory usage. A dense grid grows cubically with resolution, meaning that doubling the resolution increases storage requirements by roughly a factor of eight. As a result, voxel grids are often used as intermediate representations rather than final output models.

\paragraph{3D Gaussian splatting.}

A more recent representation is 3D Gaussian splatting~\cite{kerbl_3d_2023}. Instead of points or triangles, a scene is represented as many small 3D Gaussian primitives. Each primitive has a position, shape, opacity and colour model, resulting in a collection of blurry disks that form a view dependent representation of a scene.

This representation has become popular because it can generate realistic new viewpoints in real time while preserving high visual quality. It combines approximate geometry and appearance in a compact form.

However, Gaussian splatting is currently more suitable for visualization than for editing. Reliable object-level manipulation, topology-aware reconstruction and geometry completion remain active research topics~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025, yin_sai3d_2023}. For this reason, it is not used as an intermediate or final representation in this thesis.

\subsection{Implicit Representations}
\label{sec:bg_implicit}

Implicit representations do not store surfaces directly. Instead, geometry is described through functions evaluated in space. The surface is then recovered from these functions. Such methods are often more flexible than explicit representations and are widely used in reconstruction and generative modelling (Figure~\ref{fig:2_Implicit_Reps}).

\paragraph{Signed Distance Functions.}

A \gls{SDF} assigns to every point in space a value equal to its distance to the nearest surface. By convention, values are negative inside an object, positive outside, and zero exactly on the surface:
\[
f(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \quad \text{on the surface}.
\]

The surface can therefore be recovered as the zero level set of the function. This gives a smooth and continuous description of geometry.

\gls{SDF}s can be rendered using different techniques. One way is through ray marching, a technique that traces rays step-by-step from the viewpoint, using the function to determine the distance to the surface as the length of each marching step \cite{hart_sphere_1996}. Alternatively, a mesh can be generated from the function by using algorithms like Marching Cubes~\cite{lorensen_marching_1998}, which generates polygons at discrete points in space where the surface distance is close to zero.

When sampled on a voxel grid, SDFs are well suited for learning-based completion methods and have been widely used for object reconstruction~\cite{mittal_autosdf_2022, ren_xcube_2023, cheng_sdfusion_2023}. However, standard SDFs assume a closed watertight surface, which is often not available in raw scan data. \gls{UDF}s avoid this limitation by storing only unsigned distance values~\cite{vermandere_geometry_2025}.

\paragraph{Neural implicit representations.}

Neural implicit methods use neural networks to represent geometry or appearance as continuous functions. A well-known example is the \gls{NeRF}, which maps spatial position and viewing direction to density and colour~\cite{mildenhall_nerf_2020}.

These methods can produce highly realistic novel-view synthesis results and compactly encode complex scenes. However, the scene structure is stored inside network parameters rather than as directly editable objects. This makes object-level interaction, deterministic updates and geometric editing more difficult~\cite{weber_nerfiller_2023}.

Recent work aims to decompose such models into separate objects~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025}, but robust interactive editing is still an open challenge. Neural implicit methods are therefore considered useful supporting tools, but not final output formats for the dynamic scene representations targeted in this thesis.

\subsection{Representations used in This Thesis}
\label{sec:bg_rep_choice}

Given the above considerations, the pipeline developed in this thesis operates primarily with coloured point clouds and textured meshes as the main data types, with SDFs and UDFs as intermediate representations in completion stages. Point clouds serve as the natural output of the capture devices used and as the input to detection and completion modules~\cite{qi_pointnet_2016}. Textured meshes serve as the output of the scene completion stage, providing a compact, renderable and editable surface representation~\cite{vermandere_semantic_2024}. SDFs are used internally within object completion networks, bridging the gap between the irregular scan data and the fixed-size inputs required by learning-based models~\cite{mittal_autosdf_2022, chibane_implicit_2020}.

\section{Dynamic Reality Model Requirements}
\label{sec:bg_drm_requirements}

Having established the representational landscape, we can now formalise what a \gls{DRM} must provide and what inputs it operates on. This section defines the scope and structural requirements of the proposed representation.

% ── 2.3.1 ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Inputs}
\label{sec:bg_inputs}

The pipeline takes as input heterogeneous, multi-source data captured from real indoor environments. This data is inherently incomplete, as no single scan captures a scene in its entirety. Two primary modalities are considered.

\paragraph{Point clouds.}
Unstructured point clouds form the geometric backbone of the input data. These may originate from high-end terrestrial laser scanners, which provide millimetre-level accuracy over large ranges, or from lower-end mobile and \gls{XR}-based devices with lower accuracy, but faster acquisition speeds. These scan can optionally contain per-point colour information. Multiple scans can be used of the same scene, captured at different times or with different devices, to provide complementary coverage and to enable temporal updates.

\paragraph{Image data.}
Panoramic images or localised pinhole camera images provide complementary appearance information. Panoramic images, captured at the same positions as the scan data, enable dense colour assignment to the point cloud through angular projection. Localised images from mobile devices provide additional texture cues and can support 2D-based alignment and inpainting operations. The co-localisation of images within the same coordinate system as the point cloud is a prerequisite for cross-modal processing~\cite{bassier_geomapi_2024}.

% ── 2.3.2 ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Output Requirements}
\label{sec:bg_outputs}

The output of the pipeline is a \gls{DRM}: a structured, complete and interactive representation of the indoor environment. This representation must satisfy the following requirements.

\paragraph{Geometric completeness.}
Both the structural elements of the scene and the individual objects within it must be geometrically complete. Occluded or unobserved regions must be reconstructed so that the resulting representation does not contain holes or missing surfaces. This applies at two levels: the background scene, consisting of walls, floors and ceilings, and the individual foreground objects, such as furniture or equipment.

It is useful to distinguish three categories of occlusion that the pipeline must address. \textit{Sensor occlusion} refers to regions that fall outside the scanner's line of sight entirely, such as the back of a room not covered by any scan position. \textit{Inter-object occlusion} refers to surfaces of one object that are hidden behind another, such as the rear face of a shelf visible only if the objects in front of it are removed. \textit{Contact occlusion} refers to surfaces hidden because two objects are physically touching, such as the base of a chair leg resting on a floor or the back of a cabinet flush against a wall. Each category requires a different treatment: sensor occlusions can often be resolved by additional scan positions or scene-level inference~\cite{roldao_3d_2022}, inter-object occlusions require object-level reasoning~\cite{vermandere_geometry_2025}, and contact occlusions require knowledge of both the object geometry and the surrounding scene structure~\cite{vermandere_guided_2025}. The occlusion analysis developed in Chapter~\ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation} explicitly estimates all three types and passes this information to the scene and object completion stages.

\paragraph{Visual completeness.}
The completed geometry must be accompanied by plausible texture. Missing texture regions that arise from occlusions or incomplete image coverage must be reconstructed in a manner that is consistent with the surrounding appearance. The resulting textured representation must be visually coherent and plausible across the entire scene.

\paragraph{Object-level decomposition.}
Individual objects must be represented as separate entities within the scene, rather than as part of a monolithic surface. This decomposition is essential for enabling object-level manipulation. Each object must support a set of basic manipulation operations: repositioning within the scene, removal from the scene, and scaling. These operations must be applicable without re-running the full pipeline and without introducing visual artefacts in the surrounding scene.

\paragraph{Part awareness and scalability.}
To support realistic manipulation, completed objects must carry a notion of their internal part structure. This enables part-aware scaling operations, in which individual structural components of an object — such as the legs or seat of a chair, or the shelves of a bookcase — can be scaled independently or repeated, rather than applying a uniform deformation to the entire object. Uniform scaling of complex objects frequently produces geometrically implausible results by distorting repeating structural elements; part-aware scaling preserves the proportionality of individual components while adapting the overall dimensions~\cite{vermandere_adaptive_2026, wei_approximate_2022}.

\section{Pipeline Overview}
\label{sec:bg_pipeline_overview}

The requirements defined above are addressed by a modular pipeline, each stage of which is developed in detail in subsequent chapters. This section provides the conceptual description of each stage and its role within the overall workflow, fulfilling the pointer made in Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction}. A key design decision running through the pipeline is the parallel treatment of scene and object completion, which is motivated here before the individual stages are described (Figure~\ref{fig:2_Main_Pipeline}).

\subsection{The Scene-to-Object Completion Duality}
\label{sec:bg_duality}

A central challenge in producing complete dynamic scenes is that scene-level and object-level completion are mutually dependent yet conventionally treated as separate problems. Scene-level completion methods~\cite{roldao_3d_2022, dai_scancomplete_2018, weber_nerfiller_2023} infer missing geometry by reasoning over the environment as a whole. This works well for regions that were simply outside the scanner's field of view, but fails at contact occlusions: surfaces hidden because two objects are physically touching cannot be distinguished from empty space by a global volumetric model, which has no concept of object identity or boundary.

Object-level completion methods~\cite{mittal_autosdf_2022, chibane_implicit_2020, ren_xcube_2023, zhou_3d_2021} operate on objects after they have been isolated from the scene. Isolation, however, strips away the environmental context that is most informative for completion. The floor surface constrains the bottom extent of a chair; the wall behind a cabinet defines its maximum depth; a neighbouring object provides an upper bound on where one object can end and another begin. A completion network that operates on a centred, decontextualised object mesh has none of this information available~\cite{vermandere_guided_2025}.

The proposed pipeline resolves this mutual dependency by decoupling detection from completion while keeping both coupled through a shared geometric analysis. Object detection produces bounding boxes that are refined using scene geometry — walls, floors and symmetry axes — before any completion is attempted~\cite{vermandere_guided_2025, sipiran_approximate_2014}. A voxel-based occlusion analysis then produces separate grids at the object level and scene level~\cite{vermandere_synthetic_2026}. These grids encode which regions are genuinely missing versus which were simply not observed, and they are passed simultaneously to the scene completion stage, which uses them to avoid reconstructing geometry in regions occupied by detected objects, and to the object completion stage, which uses them to define the voxels that the completion network must predict~\cite{vermandere_geometry_2025}. The result is a pair of complementary reconstructions that are geometrically consistent with one another from the outset.

\subsection{Pipeline Stages}
\label{sec:bg_stages}

\paragraph{Scene alignment and updating.}
Before any completion or decomposition can take place, multi-source and multi-temporal data must be brought into a common coordinate system. This stage handles the registration of point clouds and images from heterogeneous devices and integrates incremental updates from lower-end sensors into existing high-quality datasets~\cite{vermandere_two-step_2022}. The output is a consistently georeferenced, multi-modal dataset representing the current state of the environment~\cite{bassier_geomapi_2024}. This stage is addressed in Chapter~\ref{ch:4_Alignment_Updating}.

\paragraph{Object detection and occlusion analysis.}
The aligned and combined point cloud is decomposed into individual objects using 3D detection methods~\cite{qi_deep_2019}. Each detected object is associated with an oriented bounding box that is refined using symmetry estimation~\cite{sipiran_approximate_2014} and scene geometry constraints such as walls and floors. In parallel, a voxel-based occlusion analysis estimates the occluded regions of both individual objects and the full scene, distinguishing the three occlusion categories defined in Section~\ref{sec:bg_outputs}~\cite{vermandere_synthetic_2026}. The resulting grids provide the shared geometric information that couples the scene and object completion stages. This stage is addressed in Chapter~\ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation}.

\paragraph{Scene completion.}
The structural elements of the environment, separated from the detected objects, are completed geometrically and texturally. Planar structures such as walls, floors and ceilings are reconstructed using plane detection and intersection-based geometry generation~\cite{vermandere_semantic_2024}, guided by the scene-level occlusion grid to restrict reconstruction to genuinely missing regions and to avoid filling space occupied by detected objects. Texture is inpainted on the reconstructed geometry using the panoramic image data as reference~\cite{vermandere_semantic_2024, suvorov_resolution-robust_2022}. The output is a complete, textured mesh of the background scene. This stage is addressed in Chapter~\ref{ch:6_Scene_Completion}.

\paragraph{Object completion.}
Individual objects are completed independently, using both the partial point cloud and the co-registered image data as input~\cite{vermandere_geometry_2025}. The object-level occlusion grid defines which voxels the completion network must predict, while the refined bounding box — constrained by the scene planes — defines the physical extent within which the completion is permitted~\cite{vermandere_guided_2025}. The completed objects are represented as textured meshes and carry part-index information derived from material segmentation~\cite{vermandere_geometry_2025}, enabling the subsequent dynamification step. This stage is addressed in Chapter~\ref{ch:7_Object_Completion}.

\paragraph{Scene dynamification.}
The completed objects are transformed into manipulable entities by applying part-aware scaling and decomposition~\cite{wei_approximate_2022}. Each object's internal structure is analysed and divided into approximately convex components that serve as the basis for scaling zones, using the material indices produced during object completion as structural priors~\cite{vermandere_geometry_2025}. The completed scene and its objects are then assembled into a \gls{DRM} in which each object can be independently repositioned, scaled or removed. This stage is addressed in Chapter~\ref{ch:8_Scene_Dynamification}.

\section{Existing Approaches to Scene Dynamification}
\label{sec:bg_sota_dynamification}

Although the individual components of the proposed pipeline, such as object detection~\cite{qi_deep_2019}, completion~\cite{mittal_autosdf_2022, chibane_implicit_2020} and inpainting~\cite{suvorov_resolution-robust_2022, vermandere_semantic_2024}, have been studied extensively in isolation, the specific problem of transforming a partial scan into a fully dynamic and editable scene representation has received comparatively little attention~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025}.

The closest relevant body of work includes methods for indoor scene understanding that decompose a scan into structural and object components. Li~et~al.~\cite{li_hybrid_2024} segment indoor point clouds into planar structural elements and detected objects, reconstruct the structural geometry via plane fitting~\cite{korman_latent_2018}, and replace detected objects with matched CAD models retrieved from a database~\cite{siddiqui_retrievalfuse_2021}. This approach produces a lightweight and semantically structured scene, but is limited to objects that have a close match in a predefined database and does not address the visual completeness of the reconstruction.

Methods based on neural implicit representations, such as NeRFiller~\cite{weber_nerfiller_2023} and related scene completion approaches~\cite{dai_spsg_2020, chen_circle_2021}, use generative 2D inpainting to complete missing regions within neural radiance field representations. While these approaches can produce visually plausible completions, the resulting representations are not decomposed at the object level and do not support object-level manipulation or explicit geometric editing~\cite{roldao_3d_2022}.

Gen3DSR~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025} reconstructs a dynamic 3D scene from a single RGB image by separating objects from the scene using 2D segmentation masks and completing each independently using depth estimation and generative models. This is conceptually the closest existing approach to the pipeline proposed here, but it relies on a single image as input, limiting geometric accuracy, and does not address the alignment, updating or part-aware scaling stages that are central to this work.

More broadly, the field of semantic scene completion~\cite{roldao_3d_2022} aims to predict complete geometry and semantics from partial observations, but typically treats the scene as a monolithic volume and does not produce object-level decompositions suitable for interactive manipulation.

In summary, while components of the proposed pipeline exist in the literature, no existing work integrates alignment, object-level completion, scene completion and part-aware dynamification into a unified framework operating on multi-source, multi-temporal scan data. This gap motivates the contributions of the subsequent chapters.

\section{Scope and Assumptions}
\label{sec:bg_conclusion}

Having established the terminological and conceptual foundation, we conclude this chapter by summarising the scope and assumptions that govern the remainder of this thesis.

\textbf{Scene type.} The pipeline is designed for single-room indoor environments containing furniture or industrial objects. This constraint reflects the focus on densely furnished scenes where occlusions are significant but the scene structure remains largely planar. Outdoor environments, multi-room layouts and highly organic or curved architectural elements are outside the scope of this work.

\textbf{Input modalities.} The pipeline operates on unstructured point clouds and panoramic or localised images. At least one high-quality baseline scan and one set of co-registered images are assumed to be available. The pipeline is designed to accommodate additional incremental observations from lower-end devices.

\textbf{Object types.} The pipeline targets geometrically distinct, rigid objects that are at least partially observed in the scan data. Objects with Surfaces that are typically very difficult to scan such as transparent or highly reflective materials, and objects with no observable geometry are not addressed.

\textbf{Output representation.} The \gls{DRM} is represented as a textured mesh for the background scene and as coloured SDFs or textured meshes for individual objects, with associated part indices. This representation is compatible with standard rendering and game engine pipelines.

\textbf{Evaluation.} Quantitative evaluation is performed primarily on the synthetic V-Scan dataset introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:3_Data_Acquisition}~\cite{vermandere_synthetic_2026}, which provides ground-truth geometry for both the scene and individual objects. This synthetic dataset is a methodological necessity rather than a convenience: because real-world scans cannot provide ground truth for occluded regions — the surfaces that completion methods are specifically required to reconstruct — no real-world benchmark can directly assess completion quality in the regions that matter most~\cite{dai_scannet_2017, chang_matterport3d_2017}. Real-world datasets are therefore used for qualitative evaluation and to assess generalisation beyond the synthetic domain.

These assumptions and constraints are revisited in Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}, where the limitations of the proposed pipeline are discussed alongside directions for future work.
