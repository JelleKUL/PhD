\section{Existing Approaches to Scene Dynamification}
\label{sec:bg_sota_dynamification}

Although the individual components of the proposed pipeline(object detection~\cite{qi_deep_2019}, completion~\cite{mittal_autosdf_2022, chibane_implicit_2020} and inpainting~\cite{suvorov_resolution-robust_2022, vermandere_semantic_2024}) have been studied extensively in isolation, the specific problem of transforming a partial scan into a fully dynamic and editable scene representation has received comparatively little attention~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025}.

The closest relevant body of work includes methods for indoor scene understanding that decompose a scan into structural and object components. Li~et~al.~\cite{li_hybrid_2024} segment indoor point clouds into planar structural elements and detected objects, reconstruct the structural geometry via plane fitting~\cite{korman_latent_2018}, and replace detected objects with matched CAD models retrieved from a database~\cite{siddiqui_retrievalfuse_2021}. This approach produces a lightweight and semantically structured scene, but is limited to objects that have a close match in a predefined database and does not address the visual completeness of the reconstruction.

Methods based on neural implicit representations, such as NeRFiller~\cite{weber_nerfiller_2023} and related scene completion approaches~\cite{dai_spsg_2020, chen_circle_2021}, use generative 2D inpainting to complete missing regions within neural radiance field representations. While these approaches can produce visually plausible completions, the resulting representations are not decomposed at the object level and do not support object-level manipulation or explicit geometric editing~\cite{roldao_3d_2022}.

Gen3DSR~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025} reconstructs a dynamic 3D scene from a single RGB image by separating objects from the scene using 2D segmentation masks and completing each independently using depth estimation and generative models. This is conceptually the closest existing approach to the pipeline proposed here, but it relies on a single image as input, limiting geometric accuracy, and does not address the alignment, updating or part-aware scaling stages that are central to this work.

More broadly, the field of semantic scene completion~\cite{roldao_3d_2022} aims to predict complete geometry and semantics from partial observations, but typically treats the scene as a monolithic volume and does not produce object-level decompositions suitable for interactive manipulation.

In summary, while components of the proposed pipeline exist in the literature, no existing work integrates alignment, object-level completion, scene completion and part-aware dynamification into a unified framework operating on multi-source, multi-temporal scan data. This gap motivates the contributions of the subsequent chapters.

\section{3D Scene Representations}
\label{sec:bg_representations}

A clear understanding of scene dynamification requires an overview of the main ways in which 3D geometry and appearance can be represented digitally. The chosen representation strongly influences which operations are possible, how missing geometry can be reconstructed, how colour can be stored, and how easily a scene can be edited or interacted with afterwards. In general, 3D representations can be divided into two broad categories: \textit{explicit} and \textit{implicit} representations.

Explicit representations store geometry directly as discrete entities such as points, surfaces or volumetric elements. They are generally intuitive to interpret and well suited for visualization, rendering and geometric processing. Implicit representations encode geometry indirectly through functions defined over 3D space, where surfaces are recovered from quantities such as occupancy, density or distance values. They are particularly useful for reconstruction, shape completion and learning-based modelling. Each representation type offers distinct advantages and limitations depending on the intended application.

% ── Explicit ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Explicit Representations}
\label{sec:bg_explicit}

Explicit representations can be visualised directly in any 3D software, without the need for any extra processing. This makes them practical for rendering, editing and many downstream applications (Figure~\ref{fig:2_Explicit_Reps}).

\paragraph{Point clouds.}

A point cloud is a collection of sampled 3D points,
\[
P = \{\mathbf{p}_i\}_{i=1}^{N},
\]
where each point is represented as
\[
\mathbf{p}_i =
(x_i, y_i, z_i,\; n_{x,i}, n_{y,i}, n_{z,i},\; r_i, g_i, b_i),
\]
with $(x_i, y_i, z_i) \in \mathbb{R}^{3}$ denoting the measured 3D position, $(n_{x,i}, n_{y,i}, n_{z,i}) \in \mathbb{R}^{3}$ the associated surface normal, and $(r_i, g_i, b_i)$ the colour attributes of the point. The point cloud therefore stores both geometric and appearance information sampled from visible surfaces.

Point clouds are the natural output of laser scanners, depth cameras and mobile sensing devices, and form the main raw input used throughout this thesis. Their main advantage is that they closely reflect real physical measurements. They are simple to acquire, scalable to large scenes and can capture fine geometric detail when densely sampled.

A distinction is commonly made between \textit{structured} and \textit{unstructured} point clouds. Structured point clouds preserve the original sensor topology, meaning that points are arranged on a regular 2D grid corresponding to image pixels or scan angles. This neighbourhood structure enables efficient operations such as normal estimation, segmentation and projection-based processing. Depth images and many RGB-D camera outputs are typical examples of structured point clouds. Unstructured point clouds, by contrast, store points only as an unordered set of spatial samples without an inherent neighbourhood relationship. They are the dominant output of terrestrial laser scanners and merged multi-scan datasets, but require spatial indexing methods such as k-d trees or octrees \todo{where explain?} to recover local neighbourhoods for processing.

However, point clouds do not contain explicit surface connectivity. In other words, neighbouring points are not directly linked to form surfaces. This makes tasks such as rendering, mesh generation or watertight reconstruction more difficult. Their quality also depends strongly on the sensing device used, ranging from highly accurate terrestrial laser scanners to noisier mobile and \gls{XR}-based systems~\cite{bassier_standalone_2016, vermandere_two-step_2022, de_geyter_automated_2022}.

\paragraph{Meshes.}

A mesh represents a surface using connected vertices, edges and faces. In practice, most meshes consist of triangles, making them efficient to process and render. A mesh can be written as
\[
M = (V,E,F),
\]
where
\[
V = \{\mathbf{v}_i\}_{i=1}^{N_v}, \qquad
\mathbf{v}_i = (x_i,y_i,z_i),
\]
is the set of vertices,
\[
E = \{(\mathbf{v}_i,\mathbf{v}_j)\},
\]
is the set of edges connecting neighbouring vertices, and
\[
F = \{(\mathbf{v}_i,\mathbf{v}_j,\mathbf{v}_k)\},
\]
is the set of triangular faces.

Meshes are the standard representation in computer graphics because they explicitly define surfaces and support efficient rendering. They are also widely used for simulation, collision detection and geometric editing.

Colour can be stored per vertex or face, but is often stored separately as a texture image. Each point on the mesh surface is linked to a location in a 2D image through \gls{UV} coordinates~\cite{heckbert_survey_1986}. For a vertex $\mathbf{v}_i$, the associated texture coordinate is written as
\[
\mathbf{t}_i = (u_i, v_i), \qquad (u_i,v_i)\in[0,1]^2,
\]
where $u_i$ and $v_i$ denote the horizontal and vertical coordinates within the texture image. The mapping from 3D surface points to 2D texture space is commonly referred to as UV mapping. This allows the 3D object to appear more detailed and realistic, without having to add more geometric complexity. However, this also introduces extra complexity when the mesh needs to be repaired or completed~\cite{vermandere_semantic_2024}.

Mesh representations from indoor scans are often reconstructed using a meshing algorithm. Since this process is not perfect, the results contain defects such as holes, incorrect connections or non-manifold geometry. Extra repair steps are therefore commonly required before further use~\cite{geyter_point_2022, attene_lightweight_2010}.

\paragraph{3D Gaussian splatting.}

A representation that has seen a recent resurgence is 3D Gaussian splatting~\cite{kerbl_3d_2023}, in which the scene is represented as a set of anisotropic 3D Gaussians,
\[
\mathcal{G} = \{G_i\}_{i=1}^{N},
\]
where each Gaussian primitive is parameterised as
\[
G_i = (\boldsymbol{\mu}_i,\Sigma_i,\alpha_i,\mathbf{s}_i).
\]
Here, $\boldsymbol{\mu}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{3}$ denotes the centre position, $\Sigma_i \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ the covariance matrix controlling orientation and spatial extent, $\alpha_i \in [0,1]$ the opacity, and $\mathbf{s}_i$ the spherical harmonic coefficients encoding view-dependent colour.

This representation has gained considerable attention for its ability to achieve real-time novel view synthesis at high visual fidelity. Each Gaussian combines approximate geometry and appearance in a compact form.

However, Gaussian splatting is currently more suitable for visualization than for editing. Reliable object-level manipulation, topology-aware reconstruction and geometry completion remain active research topics~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025, yin_sai3d_2023}. Furthermore, the lack of an explicit, watertight surface definition makes completion and UV-based texture operations difficult to apply. For these reasons, 3D Gaussian splatting is not adopted as an intermediate or output representation in this thesis.

\subsection{Implicit Representations}
\label{sec:bg_implicit}

Implicit representations do not store surfaces directly. Instead, geometry is described through functions evaluated in space. The surface is then recovered from these functions. Such methods are often more flexible than explicit representations and are widely used in reconstruction and generative modelling (Figure~\ref{fig:2_Implicit_Reps}).

\paragraph{Signed Distance Functions.}

A \gls{SDF} assigns to every point in space a value equal to its signed distance to the nearest surface. By convention, values are negative inside an object, positive outside, and zero exactly on the surface:

\begin{gather}
f(\mathbf{x}) < 0 \quad \text{inside the object}. \\
f(\mathbf{x}) = 0 \quad \text{on the surface}. \\
f(\mathbf{x}) > 0 \quad \text{outside the object}.
\end{gather}

The surface can therefore be recovered as the zero level set of the function, giving a smooth and continuous description of geometry.

3D SDFs can be rendered through ray marching, a technique that traces rays step-by-step from the viewpoint, using the function to determine the step length toward the surface~\cite{hart_sphere_1996}. Alternatively, a mesh can be extracted using the Marching Cubes algorithm~\cite{lorensen_marching_1998}, which generates triangles at the zero crossing of the discrete voxel grid.

When sampled on a regular voxel grid, SDFs are well suited for learning-based completion methods and have been widely used for object reconstruction~\cite{mittal_autosdf_2022, ren_xcube_2023, cheng_sdfusion_2023, rao_patchcomplete_2022, chu_diffcomplete_2023, yan_shapeformer_2023}. However, standard SDFs assume a closed watertight surface, which is often not available in raw scan data. \gls{UDF}s avoid this limitation by storing only unsigned distance values, making them applicable to open or incomplete meshes~\cite{vermandere_geometry_2025}.

For the dynamification step, SDFs can be extended to \gls{CSDF}s by additionally storing per-voxel colour information, enabling joint geometry and appearance manipulation~\cite{oechsle_texture_2019}.

\paragraph{Parametric and semantic models.}

Parametric and semantic models describe geometry through structured object definitions rather than purely through sampled surfaces. Instead of storing only points or triangles, these representations encode objects by parameters, construction logic and semantic relationships. They are widely used in design, engineering and manufacturing workflows because they support efficient editing, querying and procedural modification.

Computer-Aided Design (CAD) models are the most common example of parametric modelling. CAD systems typically represent objects using boundary representations, constructive solid geometry, extrusion profiles or feature-based modelling operations. For example, a table leg may be defined by a profile sketch and extrusion height rather than by a fixed triangle mesh. This allows dimensions to be changed while preserving design intent. CAD models are therefore highly suitable for manufacturing, tolerance control and geometric editing, but often contain limited semantic context about how objects function within a larger environment.

\gls{BIM} extends this principle to the building scale by representing environments as structured collections of semantically meaningful parametric objects. Each element is described by its type, dimensions and relationships to other elements, rather than only by explicit surface geometry.

This structured representation supports efficient querying, clash detection, scheduling, simulation and modification. However, CAD and BIM models typically describe intended or designed geometry rather than captured as-built conditions, and therefore often lack the geometric irregularities, clutter and visual realism present in real environments.

\paragraph{Neural implicit representations.}

Neural implicit methods use neural networks to represent geometry or appearance as continuous functions over space. Instead of storing explicit vertices, faces or points, a neural network with parameters $\theta$ learns a mapping
\[
f_{\theta} : \mathbb{R}^{3} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{m},
\]
where the input is a spatial coordinate $\mathbf{x}=(x,y,z)$ and the output may encode occupancy, distance, density, colour or other scene properties.

A well-known example is the \gls{NeRF}, which additionally conditions on viewing direction $\mathbf{d}$ and predicts volume density and emitted colour:
\[
F_{\theta}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{d}) = (\sigma,\mathbf{c}),
\]
where $\sigma$ denotes volumetric density and $\mathbf{c}=(r,g,b)$ the view-dependent radiance~\cite{mildenhall_nerf_2020}. Rendering is then performed through volumetric integration along camera rays.

These methods can produce highly realistic novel-view synthesis results and compactly encode complex scenes. However, the scene structure is stored inside the network parameters $\theta$ rather than as directly editable objects, making object-level interaction, deterministic updates and geometric editing more difficult~\cite{weber_nerfiller_2023}. Recent work aims to decompose such models into separate objects~\cite{ardelean_gen3dsr_2025}, but robust interactive editing remains an open challenge. Neural implicit methods are therefore considered useful supporting tools, but not final output formats for the dynamic scene representations targeted in this thesis.

\subsection{Spatial Data Structures}
\label{sec:bg_datastructures}

Beyond geometric representations themselves, efficient processing of 3D data also depends on the underlying data structures used to store, index and query spatial information. Large-scale scenes often contain millions of points or volumetric samples, making direct linear access computationally inefficient. Spatial data structures therefore play a central role in neighbourhood search, collision queries, ray traversal, completion and hierarchical reconstruction.

\paragraph{Voxel grids.}

Voxel grids divide 3D space into small cubic cells, analogous to how pixels divide a 2D image. A voxel grid can be written as
\[
G = \{g_{ijk}\}, \qquad i,j,k \in \{1,\dots,n\},
\]
where each voxel $g_{ijk}$ stores one or more attributes associated with that spatial cell.

Voxel grids can function as an \textit{explicit} representation when they directly encode occupancy, colour or semantic labels. For example,
\[
g_{ijk} \in \{0,1\}
\]
may indicate whether a voxel is empty or occupied. In this form, the geometry is represented explicitly as filled volume elements.

They can also function as a discretised container for \textit{implicit} representations. Instead of storing occupancy, each voxel may store a sampled scalar field such as a signed or unsigned distance value,
\[
g_{ijk} = f(x_i,y_j,z_k),
\]
where $f$ is an SDF or UDF evaluated at the voxel centre. Surfaces are then recovered as level sets of the stored field rather than from occupied cells directly. This dual role makes voxel grids particularly important in learning-based completion methods, where they provide a regular tensor structure for 3D convolutional networks~\cite{dai_scancomplete_2018, dai_spsg_2020}.

The main limitation of dense voxel grids is memory consumption. If the resolution doubles along each axis, the total number of voxels increases by a factor of eight. As a result, voxel grids are often used as intermediate representations or replaced by sparse hierarchical structures.

\paragraph{Octrees.}

An octree is a hierarchical spatial partitioning structure that recursively subdivides 3D space into eight equally sized cubic children. Starting from a root node representing the full domain, subdivision continues only in regions requiring additional detail. This adaptive refinement greatly reduces memory usage compared with dense voxel grids when large empty regions are present.

Formally, an octree node at depth $d$ represents a cubic region $\Omega_d$, which may either remain a leaf node or be subdivided into eight child regions:
\[
\Omega_d \rightarrow \{\Omega_{d+1}^{1}, \dots, \Omega_{d+1}^{8}\}.
\]

Octrees are widely used for point cloud indexing, occupancy mapping, multiresolution reconstruction and ray tracing. They are particularly suitable for indoor scenes, where much of the volume is empty space and detail is concentrated near surfaces.

\paragraph{k-d trees.}

A k-d tree (short for k-dimensional tree) is a binary space-partitioning structure used primarily for point-based data. Rather than subdividing space uniformly, each node recursively splits the dataset along one coordinate axis. For a 3D point set, successive splits are typically performed along the $x$, $y$ and $z$ axes.

At each node, a point set $P$ is partitioned into two subsets:
\[
P = P^{-} \cup P^{+},
\]
according to whether points lie below or above a splitting plane.

k-d trees are especially effective for nearest-neighbour and radius-search queries, making them a standard acceleration structure for unstructured point clouds. Operations such as normal estimation, registration, clustering and segmentation frequently rely on repeated local neighbourhood searches, for which k-d trees provide substantial speed improvements over brute-force methods.

\paragraph{Role in this thesis.}

The thesis primarily uses unstructured point clouds as input data, for which k-d trees and octrees provide efficient neighbourhood and occupancy queries during alignment, segmentation and occlusion analysis. Dense or sparse voxel grids are used as intermediate structures in completion stages, where their regular layout is advantageous for volumetric reasoning and learning-based inference. Spatial data structures therefore complement geometric representations by enabling scalable processing of large indoor datasets.

\subsection{Representations Used in This Thesis}
\label{sec:bg_rep_choice}

Given the above considerations, the pipeline operates primarily with coloured point clouds and textured meshes as the main data types, with SDFs and UDFs as intermediate representations in completion stages. Point clouds are the natural output of the sensing devices used and serve as input to detection and completion modules~\cite{qi_pointnet_2016}. Textured meshes are the output of the scene completion stage, providing a compact, renderable and editable surface representation~\cite{vermandere_semantic_2024}. SDFs are used internally within object completion networks, bridging the gap between irregular scan data and the fixed-size inputs required by learning-based models~\cite{mittal_autosdf_2022, chibane_implicit_2020}.

BIM and neural representations are excluded from the pipeline output: BIM lacks the visual fidelity and geometric detail of captured data~\cite{bassier_processing_2020}, and NeRFs do not support the deterministic object-level editing required for dynamification~\cite{weber_nerfiller_2023}. Gaussian splatting is likewise excluded due to the absence of mature tools for geometric editing and completion~\cite{kerbl_3d_2023}.

\section{Dynamic Reality Model Requirements}
\label{sec:bg_drm_requirements}

Having established the representational landscape, this section formalises what a \gls{DRM} must provide and what inputs it operates on. The requirements connect the representational choices above to the pipeline design and occlusion typology established in Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction}.

% ── 2.3.1 ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Inputs}
\label{sec:bg_inputs}

The pipeline takes as input heterogeneous, multi-source data captured from real indoor environments. No single scan captures a scene in its entirety, so the input is inherently incomplete. Two primary modalities are considered (\ref{fig:Pointcloud_Pano}).

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \medskip
  \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Pointcloud_Pano}
  \caption[Pointcloud and panoramic images]{Example of the input data: an unstructured point cloud (left) and a panoramic image (right) captured at the same scanner position. The point cloud provides geometric information, while the panoramic image provides complementary appearance information that can be projected onto the point cloud for colour assignment.}
  \label{fig:Pointcloud_Pano}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Point clouds.}
Unstructured point clouds form the geometric backbone of the input data. These may originate from high-end terrestrial laser scanners, which provide millimetre-level accuracy over large ranges, or from lower-end mobile and \gls{XR}-based devices with lower accuracy but faster acquisition speeds. Per-point colour information may optionally be present. Multiple scans of the same scene can be captured at different times or with different devices to provide complementary coverage and to enable temporal updates~\cite{vermandere_two-step_2022, vermandere_automatic_2022}.

\paragraph{Image data.}
Panoramic images or localised pinhole camera images provide complementary appearance information. Panoramic images, captured at the scanner positions, enable dense colour assignment to the point cloud through angular projection. Localised images from mobile devices provide additional texture cues and support 2D-based alignment and inpainting operations. Co-localisation of images within the same coordinate system as the point cloud is a prerequisite for cross-modal processing~\cite{bassier_geomapi_2024}.

% ── 2.3.2 ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Output Requirements}
\label{sec:bg_outputs}

The output of the pipeline is a \gls{DRM}: a complete and editable representation of the indoor environment. This representation must satisfy the following requirements.

\paragraph{Geometric completeness.}

Both the fixed structural elements of the scene and the individual objects within it must be geometrically complete. Occluded or unobserved regions must be reconstructed so that the resulting representation contains no holes, missing surfaces or incomplete volumes. This requirement applies across multiple categories of indoor elements, which are treated differently throughout the pipeline.

To distinguish between the types of elements in a scene, we use the \gls{CI/SFB} code \cite{2ILFXM_2024}, where building elements are divided in primary, secondary and tertiary categories.

Primary building elements comprise the permanent structural fabric of the environment, such as walls, floors, ceilings, columns, beams and stair cores. These components define the global enclosure and spatial layout of the room and are therefore handled by the \textit{environment completion} stage. Secondary building elements comprise fixed but typically non-structural architectural components such as doors, windows, partition walls, façade panels, radiators, built-in cabinets and suspended ceilings. Depending on their permanence and geometric integration with the surrounding structure, these elements may be reconstructed either as part of the environment or as separate editable objects. Tertiary elements comprise movable or replaceable interior assets such as furniture, machinery, shelving, desks, chairs and equipment. These elements are treated by the \textit{object completion} stage, where they are reconstructed as individually manipulable entities.

The distinction between environment and object completion is motivated by both geometry and intended interaction. Elements that define the room envelope or are permanently integrated into the building are most efficiently reconstructed as part of a continuous background model. Movable or replaceable elements instead require independent representations that support repositioning, removal or scaling after reconstruction.

\paragraph{Textural completeness.}
The completed geometry must be accompanied by plausible texture. Missing texture regions arising from occlusions or incomplete image coverage must be inpainted consistently with the surrounding appearance~\cite{vermandere_semantic_2024, suvorov_resolution-robust_2022}. The resulting textured representation must be visually coherent across the entire scene.

\paragraph{Object-level decomposition.}
Individual objects must be represented as separate entities rather than as part of a monolithic surface. This decomposition is essential for enabling object-level manipulation.

\paragraph{Part awareness and scalability.}
To support realistic manipulation, completed objects must carry a notion of their internal part structure. This enables part-aware scaling operations in which individual structural components can be scaled independently or repeated rather than applying a uniform deformation to the entire object.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:bg_metrics}

The output requirements formalised in the previous section demand geometric completeness, textural completeness, correct object detection and part-aware editability. Assessing whether a given method satisfies these requirements requires quantitative metrics that are appropriate for each output modality. Rather than introducing these metrics separately in each experimental chapter, this section defines them once in a unified framework. The metrics are organised by type: geometric metrics that assess spatial accuracy, overlap metrics that assess volumetric or categorical agreement, detection metrics that assess identification performance, and perceptual metrics that assess visual quality.

% ── Geometric ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Geometric Metrics}
\label{sec:bg_metrics_geometric}

\paragraph{Chamfer Distance.}
The Chamfer Distance (CD) measures the average closest-point distance between two point sets and is the most widely used metric for evaluating 3D shape reconstruction quality. Given two point sets $P$ and $Q$, the Chamfer Distance is defined as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:chamfer}
\text{CD}(P, Q) = \frac{1}{|P|} \sum_{p \in P} \min_{q \in Q} \|p - q\|^2 \;+\; \frac{1}{|Q|} \sum_{q \in Q} \min_{p \in P} \|q - p\|^2
\end{equation}

The first term measures the \textit{accuracy} of the prediction, while the second term measures its \textit{completeness}. A lower Chamfer Distance indicates a better geometric match. The metric is symmetric and captures both missing and excessive geometry, but it is sensitive to outliers and does not account for surface normals or topology.

In this thesis, the Chamfer Distance is used to evaluate the geometric accuracy of completed objects (Chapter~\ref{ch:7_Object_Completion}) and reconstructed scene elements (Chapter~\ref{ch:6_Scene_Completion}).

% ── Overlap ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Overlap Metrics}
\label{sec:bg_metrics_overlap}

\paragraph{Intersection over Union.}
The Intersection over Union (IoU) measures the ratio of shared space to total occupied space between a prediction and a ground-truth reference. In its general form, given two spatial regions $A$ and $B$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:iou}
\text{IoU}(A, B) = \frac{|A \cap B|}{|A \cup B|}
\end{equation}

An IoU of 1 indicates perfect agreement, while 0 indicates no overlap. This formulation applies across multiple contexts in this thesis. For \textit{volumetric object comparison}, $A$ and $B$ represent predicted and ground-truth volumes discretised as voxel occupancy grids or signed distance fields, and $|\cdot|$ denotes the number of occupied voxels. Unlike the Chamfer Distance, which operates on surface points, the volumetric IoU captures how well the interior volume of an object is preserved, making it particularly suitable for evaluating completion methods that operate on voxelised representations (Chapter~\ref{ch:7_Object_Completion}). For \textit{3D object detection}, the same formula is applied to oriented bounding boxes rather than voxel grids. A detection is typically considered correct if the IoU with the ground-truth bounding box exceeds a predefined threshold, commonly 0.25 or 0.5 (Chapter~\ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation}).

% ── Detection ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Detection Performance Metrics}
\label{sec:bg_metrics_detection}

\paragraph{Precision and Recall.}
Precision and recall characterise the trade-off between false positives and false negatives in detection tasks. Given a set of detections, each classified as a true positive (TP), false positive (FP), or false negative (FN) based on the IoU threshold defined above:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:precision_recall}
\text{Precision} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FP}}, \qquad
\text{Recall} = \frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP} + \text{FN}}
\end{equation}

Precision measures the proportion of detections that correspond to actual objects, while recall measures the proportion of actual objects that are successfully detected. A high precision with low recall indicates conservative detection with few false alarms but many missed objects; the converse indicates aggressive detection. In this thesis, precision and recall are reported for the 3D object detection stage (Chapter~\ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation}) to assess how reliably objects are identified and localised within the scene.

% ── Perceptual ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Perceptual and Image Quality Metrics}
\label{sec:bg_metrics_perceptual}

The texture completion and inpainting stages of the pipeline produce visual outputs whose quality cannot be fully captured by geometric metrics alone. Four complementary metrics are used, progressing from pixel-level accuracy to increasingly perceptually meaningful comparisons.

\paragraph{Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio.}
The Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) quantifies the pixel-level reconstruction accuracy of a predicted image $\hat{I}$ relative to a reference image $I$. It is defined in terms of the Mean Squared Error (MSE):

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:psnr}
\text{PSNR}(\hat{I}, I) = 10 \cdot \log_{10} \left( \frac{L^2}{\text{MSE}(\hat{I}, I)} \right)
\end{equation}

where $L$ is the maximum possible pixel value (typically 255 for 8-bit images) and $\text{MSE}(\hat{I}, I) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (\hat{I}_i - I_i)^2$. Higher PSNR values indicate better reconstruction quality. While PSNR is widely used due to its simplicity, it correlates poorly with human perception of image quality, as it weights all pixel differences equally regardless of their visual significance.

\paragraph{Structural Similarity Index.}
The Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM)~\cite{wang_image_2004} addresses the limitations of PSNR by comparing images based on luminance, contrast, and structural information rather than raw pixel differences. For two image patches $x$ and $y$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ssim}
\text{SSIM}(x, y) = \frac{(2\mu_x \mu_y + c_1)(2\sigma_{xy} + c_2)}{(\mu_x^2 + \mu_y^2 + c_1)(\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2 + c_2)}
\end{equation}

where $\mu_x$, $\mu_y$ are the local means, $\sigma_x^2$, $\sigma_y^2$ the local variances, $\sigma_{xy}$ the cross-covariance, and $c_1$, $c_2$ stabilisation constants. SSIM values range from $-1$ to $1$, with 1 indicating identical images. The metric is computed locally over sliding windows and averaged across the image. SSIM better reflects perceived visual quality than PSNR, particularly for distortions that affect image structure rather than pixel intensity.

\paragraph{Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity.}
The Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS)~\cite{zhang_unreasonable_2018} measures the perceptual distance between two images using deep features extracted from a pretrained neural network. Given images $\hat{I}$ and $I$, features are extracted at multiple layers $l$ of a network (typically VGG or AlexNet), and the distance is computed as a weighted sum of normalised feature differences:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lpips}
\text{LPIPS}(\hat{I}, I) = \sum_l \frac{1}{H_l W_l} \sum_{h,w} \| w_l \odot (\hat{\phi}_l^{hw} - \phi_l^{hw}) \|^2
\end{equation}

where $\phi_l^{hw}$ and $\hat{\phi}_l^{hw}$ are the unit-normalised feature activations at spatial position $(h, w)$ in layer $l$, and $w_l$ are learned per-channel weights. Lower LPIPS values indicate greater perceptual similarity. LPIPS has been shown to correlate more strongly with human judgements of image similarity than both PSNR and SSIM, particularly for the types of distortions produced by generative models.

\paragraph{Texture Similarity.}
To assess whether the material appearance of a completed object matches its reference, a texture similarity metric based on learned feature representations is employed. Given two texture patches, feature vectors are extracted using a pretrained EfficientNet~\cite{tan_efficientnet_2019} backbone, and their similarity is measured using cosine similarity:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tex_sim}
\text{TexSim}(T_1, T_2) = \frac{f(T_1) \cdot f(T_2)}{\|f(T_1)\| \cdot \|f(T_2)\|}
\end{equation}

where $f(\cdot)$ denotes the feature extraction function. A cosine similarity of 1 indicates identical feature representations. Unlike PSNR and SSIM, which compare images at the pixel level, this metric captures higher-level material and texture properties such as pattern regularity, colour distribution, and surface characteristics. This makes it particularly suited for evaluating texture completion quality, where the completed region should exhibit the same material as the reference but need not reproduce the exact pixel pattern (Chapter~\ref{ch:7_Object_Completion}).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:bg_conclusion}
 
This chapter established the conceptual and terminological foundation for the remainder of the thesis. It addressed four aspects that the subsequent chapters build upon: the positioning of scene dynamification relative to existing work, the representational landscape of 3D data, the formal requirements of a \gls{DRM}, and the evaluation framework used to assess reconstruction quality.
 
The review of prior approaches in Section~\ref{sec:bg_sota_dynamification} showed that, although important progress has been made in object detection, scene completion, object completion and visual inpainting, these components are still largely addressed in isolation. No existing method integrates them into a unified workflow capable of producing complete, editable and object-structured scene representations from heterogeneous and multi-temporal indoor scan data.
 
The representational overview in Section~\ref{sec:bg_representations} showed that the choice of representation strongly determines which operations are feasible throughout the pipeline. Explicit representations such as point clouds and meshes remain most suitable for acquisition, rendering and editing, while implicit representations such as signed and unsigned distance fields provide a flexible intermediate form for completion and learning-based inference. Spatial data structures such as voxel grids, octrees and k-d trees complement these representations by enabling scalable processing of large 3D datasets. On this basis, the thesis adopts unstructured coloured point clouds as primary input, textured meshes as the main output for reconstructed environments, and SDF- or UDF-based volumetric encodings as intermediate representations in completion stages. BIM, neural implicit and Gaussian splatting representations are excluded from the pipeline output due to their respective limitations in visual fidelity, deterministic editability or geometric maturity.
 
The \gls{DRM} requirements formalised in Section~\ref{sec:bg_drm_requirements} define what the pipeline must produce: geometric and textural completeness, object-level decomposition, and part-aware editability. The distinction between primary, secondary and tertiary building elements --- introduced through the \gls{CI/SFB} classification --- determines how each element is routed through the pipeline: permanent structural elements are completed as part of the environment, while movable objects are reconstructed as individually manipulable entities.
 
Finally, the evaluation metrics defined in Section~\ref{sec:bg_metrics} provide a unified quantitative framework for the experimental chapters that follow. Geometric accuracy is assessed through the Chamfer Distance and volumetric IoU, detection quality through precision, recall and detection IoU, and visual quality through PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS and texture similarity. By defining these metrics here rather than in individual chapters, consistent evaluation criteria are established across all pipeline stages.
 
With this conceptual, representational and evaluative basis in place, the following chapters address the individual stages of the proposed methodology, beginning with the acquisition and generation of the datasets on which the pipeline operates.
