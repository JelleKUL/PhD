\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:da_intro}

The pipeline introduced in Chapter~\ref{ch:background} takes as input heterogeneous, partially scanned 3D data of indoor environments. Before any alignment, completion or dynamification can be performed, this data must be acquired. The quality, coverage and format of the input data directly influence every subsequent stage: a scene with severe occlusions will demand more aggressive completion; a misregistered scan will propagate errors through detection and segmentation; a dataset without ground-truth geometry cannot be used to evaluate completion methods quantitatively.

This chapter addresses the data acquisition stage of the pipeline and is structured around two complementary data sources. The first is real-world scan data, which captures authentic geometric and appearance complexity, including irregular occlusion patterns, sensor noise, and furniture arrangements that reflect actual use. The second is synthetic scan data, which sacrifices some realism in exchange for complete, verifiable ground truth — including geometry for regions that were occluded during the scan, which no real-world dataset can provide.

The scope of this chapter, and of the thesis as a whole, is limited to single-room indoor environments containing furniture or industrial equipment. This focus reflects the target application domain of the proposed pipeline and constrains the types of scenes considered. Both residential and institutional interiors are included, as are laboratory and industrial spaces with atypical object classes.

The chapter is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:da_relatedwork} reviews the landscape of capture technologies and publicly available real-world datasets, and discusses the inherent limitations of both real and synthetic data. Section~\ref{sec:da_realworld} describes the real-world data captures performed within this work and the characteristics of the publicly available datasets used for evaluation. Section~\ref{sec:da_synthetic} presents the synthetic data creation methodology and introduces the V-Scan dataset. Section~\ref{sec:da_conclusion} summarises the characteristics and limitations of the acquired data and their implications for the subsequent pipeline stages.

\section{Related Work: Capture Technologies and Datasets}
\label{sec:da_relatedwork}

% ── 3.2.1 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Capture Technologies}
\label{sec:da_capturetech}

The accurate acquisition of 3D indoor geometry has been a central research challenge in robotics, photogrammetry and computer vision for over two decades. The landscape of capture technologies spans a wide range of accuracy, speed, cost and portability trade-offs, and the choice of device has a direct effect on the type and severity of occlusions in the resulting data.

\paragraph{Terrestrial laser scanners.}
\gls{TLS} devices are static, tripod-mounted instruments that emit laser pulses in a spherical pattern from a fixed position, recording the distance and direction of each return to produce a dense, highly accurate point cloud. High-end systems such as the Leica ScanStation P30 achieve point-level accuracies below 2~mm at ranges up to 270~m, with scan rates of up to 1~million points per second~\cite{bassier_standalone_2016}. Their principal limitation in indoor settings is occlusion: because each setup covers only the hemisphere visible from a single position, any object that is not in the scanner's line of sight from at least one setup will be missing from the data. In practice, acquiring full-room coverage requires between three and ten setups per room, with each additional setup adding scanning and registration time. Furniture, equipment and the room's own structural elements all contribute to residual occlusions even with multiple setups. The resulting data is georeferenced through the use of control targets measured by total station or GNSS, and successive setups are aligned by iterative closest point or target-based registration.

\paragraph{Mobile mapping systems.}
\gls{MMS} combine laser scanning with \gls{SLAM} and \gls{IMU} data to enable continuous scanning along a trajectory, producing a large-area point cloud from a single walk-through without the need for static setups. Systems such as the NavVis VLX achieve point accuracies of around 6~mm over 500~m², at scan rates of 1.28~million points per second~\cite{noauthor_navvis_nodate}. The ability to scan from many positions along a continuous path substantially reduces inter-object occlusions compared to static TLS setups, as the scanner naturally approaches objects from multiple angles. However, trajectory drift and the lower accuracy of SLAM-based localisation compared to surveying-grade control networks introduce systematic errors at larger scales. Indoor MMS devices also typically produce panoramic imagery co-registered to the trajectory, providing appearance data alongside the geometry.

\paragraph{XR and consumer devices.}
Extended reality devices and consumer smartphones equipped with depth sensors represent the lower end of the accuracy spectrum. The Microsoft Hololens~2 integrates a \gls{ToF} depth sensor, an 8~MP colour camera, and an on-board SLAM processor, producing a real-time polygonal mesh of the environment at a default spatial resolution of 0.08~m$^3$ per cell~\cite{vermandere_two-step_2022}. Consumer smartphones such as the iPhone Pro series embed shorter-range ToF LiDAR sensors with a practical range of around 5~m and an accuracy on the order of 1--2~cm~\cite{abdel-majeed_indoor_2024}. These devices trade geometric precision for speed, portability and cost, enabling rapid partial coverage of a scene in a matter of minutes. Their lower accuracy and limited range make them unsuitable as primary acquisition devices for detailed reconstruction, but they are well suited for incremental updates of existing high-quality datasets, as exploited in the alignment methodology of Chapter~\ref{ch:4_Alignment_Updating}.

\paragraph{Photogrammetry.}
\gls{SfM} photogrammetry reconstructs 3D geometry from a set of overlapping 2D images by jointly estimating camera poses and a sparse 3D point cloud, which can be densified and meshed in post-processing. While photogrammetry is highly flexible and requires no specialised hardware beyond a camera, it is sensitive to textureless or reflective surfaces, requires dense image overlap, and produces lower geometric accuracy than dedicated LiDAR systems without carefully placed control points. In the context of this thesis, photogrammetry is primarily used as a source of localised imagery for alignment and texture reference, rather than as the primary geometry acquisition modality.

% ── 3.2.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Limitations of Real and Synthetic Data}
\label{sec:da_limits}

Both real and synthetic data sources present characteristic limitations that are relevant to the proposed pipeline.

Real-world datasets offer authentic geometric and appearance complexity, but they cannot provide ground truth for occluded regions. A scanned point cloud records only what was visible to the sensor; the surfaces behind furniture, under objects and inside cavities are structurally absent from the data. This means that any evaluation of a completion method using only real-world data is limited to assessing the visual plausibility of the result, not its geometric accuracy. Furthermore, real-world datasets require significant capture effort, and the resulting data is heterogeneous in terms of device, acquisition conditions and annotation quality across different scenes.

Synthetic datasets offer complete control over scene geometry, object placement, occlusion configuration and sensor simulation. Because every surface in the virtual scene is known, it is possible to generate both a partial scan mimicking real-world sensor behaviour and a complete ground-truth representation of the scene and its objects. This enables quantitative evaluation of completion methods in terms of geometric accuracy, not merely visual quality. The principal limitation of synthetic data is the domain gap: virtual scenes differ from real environments in lighting, material appearance, geometry complexity and object diversity, which can reduce the transferability of learned models trained exclusively on synthetic data~\cite{de_geyter_automated_2022}.

The approach adopted in this thesis uses both sources in a complementary way. Real-world data is used to validate qualitative performance and to ensure that the pipeline generalises beyond the synthetic domain. Synthetic data, specifically the V-Scan dataset introduced in Section~\ref{sec:da_synthetic}, provides the quantitative ground truth required for systematic evaluation of each pipeline stage.

\subsection{Laser Scanner Captures}
\label{sec:da_tls}

\paragraph{Static TLS captures.}
A series of real-world indoor scans were acquired at the KU Leuven Technology Campus in Ghent as part of the data collection effort underlying the alignment work in Chapter~\ref{ch:4_Alignment_Updating}. Three distinct spaces were captured over multiple time periods using a Leica ScanStation P30 and a Leica BLK360. The first space is a soil technology laboratory that resembles an industrial site, housing working desks, machinery and bulk materials. Between periodic data captures spanning several months, the lab remained operational, meaning that all movable objects were displaced between sessions. The second space is a prefab living laboratory, captured from early stake-out through to MEP installation. The third is a residential house in Brugge captured during refurbishment, with both smartphone and Hololens~2 data supplementing the TLS baseline~\cite{vermandere_two-step_2022}.

For each static TLS capture, between 2 and 20 individual scanner setups were acquired per session, with each setup producing a structured point cloud in E57 format and a set of co-registered panoramic images. Control points established by total station were used to georeference the data. The resulting point clouds span tens to hundreds of millions of points per session, with single-point accuracies below 5~mm. Static TLS captures provide the highest geometric accuracy of any modality used in this work, but their occlusion pattern is also the most severe. From a limited number of fixed positions, large portions of each room — particularly the rear faces of furniture and the regions beneath and between objects — remain unobserved. This is the primary source of incomplete geometry in the pipeline inputs and motivates the completion methodology developed in Chapters~\ref{ch:6_Scene_Completion} and~\ref{ch:7_Object_Completion}.

\paragraph{Mobile mapping captures.}
The living laboratory test case was additionally captured using a NavVis VLX indoor MMS. The MMS produced a full-room trajectory scan in a single walk-through session, yielding 162~million points with panoramic images at key trajectory positions. The higher coverage and reduced inter-object occlusion of the MMS data, compared to the static TLS setups, make it a useful reference for assessing the impact of scanner configuration on occlusion severity.

% ── 3.3.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{XR Device Captures}
\label{sec:da_xr}

The Hololens~2 was used across all three test cases to provide fast, incremental coverage updates to the existing TLS baseline datasets. The device produces a real-time polygonal mesh and a series of localised images captured during the scan session. Scan sessions are short — typically five to fifteen minutes — and can be performed without the logistical overhead of a full TLS campaign. Each session produces between 15 and 500~thousand points, depending on the room size and scan duration, at a spatial resolution of 0.08~m$^3$ and a point accuracy consistent with the ToF sensor (approximately 10~mm per 10~m of range). The panoramic and localised image streams associated with each session provide appearance data for texture assignment and 2D-based alignment.

A custom Unity-based XR capture application was developed to manage the Hololens~2 data acquisition, enabling real-time feedback on scan coverage and on-device previewing of the accumulated mesh. This application transmits captured meshes and images to a remote server for automatic alignment and integration into the reference dataset, as described in Chapter~\ref{ch:4_Alignment_Updating}. Android smartphones were used in parallel with the Hololens~2 in some sessions to provide additional image coverage for photogrammetric alignment, exploiting their higher image resolution (up to 12~MP) compared to the Hololens~2 camera (approximately 8~MP effective for XR use).

The principal limitation of XR captures for the purposes of this pipeline is range: the Hololens~2 ToF sensor saturates at distances beyond approximately 3--4~m in practice, meaning that large rooms cannot be scanned completely from a single position and that distant surfaces such as high ceilings or far walls are frequently missing from the data. This limitation makes XR data unsuitable as the primary acquisition modality for completion but appropriate for targeted incremental updates.

\paragraph{SUN RGB-D~\cite{song_sun_2015}.}
The SUN RGB-D dataset provides 10,335 \gls{RGB-D} images captured with four different sensors — Microsoft Kinect v1, Kinect v2, Intel RealSense, and Asus Xtion — across a wide variety of indoor scene types including offices, bedrooms, kitchens and classrooms. The dataset is densely annotated and includes 146,617 2D polygons and 64,595 3D bounding boxes with accurate object orientations, as well as a 3D room layout and scene category for each image. SUN RGB-D is widely used as a benchmark for 3D object detection and scene understanding, and it is used in this thesis primarily as a source of evaluation scenes for object detection methods in Chapter~\ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation}. Its use of consumer RGB-D sensors means that geometric accuracy is limited, with typical depth noise on the order of several centimetres, and occlusion patterns reflect the single-viewpoint nature of each captured frame.

\paragraph{ScanNet~\cite{dai_scannet_2017}.}
ScanNet is an RGB-D video dataset containing 2.5 million views in more than 1,500 scans, annotated with 3D camera poses, surface reconstructions, and instance-level semantic segmentations. Scenes were captured with a consumer Structure Sensor mounted on an iPad, producing reconstructed meshes via BundleFusion. The availability of textured meshes and instance-level annotations makes ScanNet a valuable resource for training and evaluating segmentation and completion methods. However, the consumer-grade sensor introduces significant depth noise and limited range, and the handheld capture trajectory results in irregular coverage with many inter-object occlusions unresolved. In this thesis ScanNet is used as a qualitative evaluation dataset for object completion and scene segmentation methods.

\paragraph{ScanNet++~\cite{yeshwanth_scannetpp_2023}.}
ScanNet++ presents a large-scale dataset coupling high-quality and commodity-level geometry of indoor scenes. Each scene is captured with a high-end laser scanner at sub-millimetre resolution, along with registered 33-megapixel images from a DSLR camera, and RGB-D streams from an iPhone. Currently, ScanNet++ contains 460 scenes, 280,000 captured DSLR images, and over 3.7M iPhone RGBD frames. The combination of laser-quality geometry and high-resolution imagery makes ScanNet++ the highest-fidelity real-world indoor dataset currently available. The semantic instance annotations further enable evaluation of detection and segmentation methods. In this thesis, ScanNet++ scenes and their pre-labelled instance annotations are used to evaluate the texture completion methodology of Chapter~\ref{ch:6_Scene_Completion}, as reported in the semantic UV mapping work~\cite{vermandere_semantic_2024}.

\paragraph{Matterport3D~\cite{chang_matterport3d_2017}.}
Matterport3D is a large-scale RGB-D dataset containing 10,800 panoramic views from 194,400 RGB-D images of 90 building-scale scenes, with annotations including surface reconstructions, camera poses, and 2D and 3D semantic segmentations. Scenes span a wide variety of building types including homes, offices and churches, and each building is captured comprehensively across all floors. The Matterport Pro Camera captures RGB-D panoramas from static positions, producing textured meshes with good overall coverage but the occlusion profile of a limited-position static scanner. Matterport3D is used extensively in this thesis for evaluation of object detection, object completion, scene completion and dynamification methods across Chapters~\ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation} through~\ref{ch:8_Scene_Dynamification}, and its pre-labelled instance masks serve as ground truth for object isolation experiments.

\paragraph{Redwood-3DScan~\cite{choi_large_2016}.}
The Redwood-3DScan dataset contains 10,933 RGB-D scans of individual objects captured by 70 non-expert operators using consumer-grade PrimeSense Carmine cameras, with 9,131 scans categorised into 320 object categories. Unlike the scene-level datasets above, Redwood focuses on individual isolated objects rather than full rooms. Each scan is a short RGB-D video sequence of an operator walking around a single object, from which a partial point cloud and optionally a reconstructed mesh are derived. This makes Redwood valuable as a source of real-world partial object scans for evaluating object completion methods, though the absence of per-object ground truth for occluded regions limits quantitative evaluation to observable surfaces only.

\subsection{Evaluation and Shortcomings of Real-World Data}
\label{sec:da_realeval}

Across all real-world datasets and captures, a consistent set of limitations affects their utility for the specific task of evaluating a completion pipeline.

The most fundamental limitation is the absence of ground truth for occluded regions. No real-world scanning campaign, however thorough, can observe the surfaces that are hidden behind or between objects. This means that evaluation of completion quality on real-world data is necessarily qualitative, assessing visual plausibility and consistency rather than geometric accuracy. Evaluating whether a completed surface is geometrically correct — rather than merely visually plausible — requires a ground-truth reference that real-world data cannot provide.

A related limitation concerns object-level ground truth. Even datasets with instance-level annotations, such as Matterport3D and ScanNet, do not provide complete 3D models of individual objects. The annotated instance masks label which points or mesh faces belong to each object, but the hidden surfaces of those objects — the parts not visible from any scan position — are absent. This makes it impossible to evaluate object completion accuracy in terms of the full 3D shape, as opposed to only the visible portion.

Additionally, real-world datasets vary considerably in scan quality, coverage and annotation consistency. Consumer RGB-D sensors introduce geometric noise that can confound completion and segmentation methods. Coverage varies with the scanner configuration: a single-position TLS scan of a cluttered room will have far more severe occlusions than a multi-position scan of the same room. These variations make it difficult to isolate the effect of individual pipeline components across different evaluation scenes.

These shortcomings collectively motivate the synthetic data approach described in the following section.

\section{Synthetic Data Creation}
\label{sec:da_synthetic}

% ── 3.4.1 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Related Work: Synthetic Datasets and Virtual Scanners}
\label{sec:da_syntheticrelated}

The use of synthetic data for training and evaluating 3D scene understanding methods has a long history. Scene-level synthetic datasets including SUNCG~\cite{song_semantic_2016}, SceneNet RGB-D~\cite{mccormac_scenenet_nodate} and InteriorNet~\cite{li_interiornet_2018} provide large collections of procedurally generated indoor scenes with RGB-D images and dense annotations. At the object level, ShapeNet~\cite{chang_shapenet_2015} and ModelNet~\cite{wu_3d_2015} provide thousands of clean 3D CAD models that have been widely used to train completion and classification networks. These datasets have driven substantial progress in 3D learning, but they share a common approach to generating partial scan data: points are sampled directly from mesh surfaces, either uniformly or from simulated viewpoints, without modelling the physical behaviour of the scanning sensor. This produces point distributions that differ systematically from those of real scanners, which measure only line-of-sight surfaces and introduce distance-dependent noise and resolution variation.

A smaller body of work has addressed physically-based virtual scanning simulation. Blume and Heimann~\cite{blume_simulating_2006} presented an early laser scanner simulator for wheeled robot navigation. HELIOS++~\cite{winiwarter_virtual_2022} is a ray-tracing-based aerial LiDAR simulator designed for terrain mapping and forestry analysis, providing accurate sensor modelling but not designed for indoor scene generation or object-level ground truth. Popovas et al.~\cite{popovas_virtual_2021} developed a virtual TLS simulator for training and educational purposes in construction contexts. None of these frameworks provides the combination of indoor procedural scene generation, configurable multi-device simulation, per-object ground truth export, and voxel-based occlusion annotation required for the evaluation tasks of this thesis.

% ── 3.4.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Methodology: The Virtual Scanner}
\label{sec:da_virtualscanner}

The virtual scanning framework is implemented in Unity and designed to simulate the behaviour of real-world scanning devices through configurable physical parameters~\cite{vermandere_synthetic_2025}. Rather than sampling points directly from mesh surfaces, the system performs ray-based scanning from a virtual viewpoint, naturally replicating the line-of-sight visibility constraints of a physical scanner. The full pipeline is summarised in Figure~\ref{fig:da_virtualscanner}.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \medskip
  %\includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{da_virtualscanner_overview}
  \caption[Virtual scanner pipeline]{Overview of the virtual scanning pipeline showing the scanner setup, ray-based acquisition, panoramic colouring, object isolation and occlusion computation stages.}
  \label{fig:da_virtualscanner}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Scanner setup and parameterisation.}
The virtual scanner is parameterised by scan density (mm per 10~m), maximum range (m), vertical field of view (degrees), system error (mm) and distance-dependent error (\%). Presets are provided that emulate four representative real-world devices: the Leica ScanStation P30 (\mbox{$\delta_r = 1.2$~mm + 10~ppm}, 1~MHz, up to 270~m), the NavVis VLX (6~mm per 500~m², 1.28~MHz, up to 50~m), the Leica BLK360 (4~mm at 10~m, 0.68~MHz, up to 45~m), and the iPhone Pro LiDAR (1--2~cm, depth sensing only, approximately 5~m range). This parameterisation allows the same scene to be scanned under multiple simulated device configurations, enabling analysis of the effect of sensor quality on downstream pipeline performance.

\paragraph{Scan vector generation.}
Scanning begins by generating a spherical array of ray directions. Rays are distributed uniformly along horizontal disks stacked at equal angular intervals in the vertical direction, starting from a configurable minimum elevation angle to model the blind zone beneath the sensor. Equal angular spacing in both directions ensures uniform angular coverage and computational efficiency compared to sequential vertical sweep patterns. The total number of rays is determined by the configured scan density and the device field of view.

\paragraph{Ray-casting and point cloud acquisition.}
Each ray is cast from the scanner origin until it intersects the collider mesh of a scene object. The intersection point, its distance from the origin and the surface normal at the intersection are recorded. Gaussian noise is applied at the magnitude of the system error parameter, and a distance-proportional error component is added to simulate the increasing measurement uncertainty at longer ranges. Points beyond the maximum range are discarded. The result is a partial, noise-affected point cloud that covers only those surfaces visible from the scanner position, accurately modelling the occlusion pattern of a real static scanner.

\paragraph{Panoramic image capture and point colouring.}
After the geometric scan is complete, a 360° panoramic image is captured at the scanner's position by rendering a cubemap from the virtual camera and converting it to an equirectangular projection. Each scanned point is then assigned a colour by projecting its 3D position into spherical coordinates and sampling the corresponding pixel in the panoramic image. This produces a coloured point cloud that encodes the lighting conditions, material appearance and textures of the virtual environment, closely matching the output of real TLS systems that capture panoramic imagery alongside their geometric data.

\paragraph{Object-level scanning and isolation.}
Each non-structural object in the scene is assigned a \texttt{ScannedObject} component that stores its oriented bounding box (OBB) and ground-truth mesh reference. After the full-scene scan, each object's partial point cloud is extracted by selecting the scanned points whose positions fall within the object's OBB, optionally expanded by a small margin to avoid clipping edge points. The object's OBB is exported as a set of eight 3D corner points in world coordinates. The ground-truth mesh is simultaneously exported from the scene, providing a complete surface representation of the object independent of the scan coverage.

\paragraph{Voxel-based occlusion computation.}
Occlusion is computed at both scene level and per-object level using a voxel-based ray-casting approach. For each target — either the full scene bounding box or an individual object's OBB — a regular voxel grid is defined at a configurable resolution. For each voxel centre, a ray is cast from the scanner origin. If the ray intersects any scene geometry before reaching the voxel centre, the voxel is marked as occluded; otherwise it is marked as free. Voxels within the object's surface are marked as occupied. The resulting sparse voxel grids, exported in a compact format, encode the ground-truth visibility information used by the occlusion analysis stage in Chapter~\ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation} to distinguish sensor occlusion, inter-object occlusion, and contact occlusion as defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:background}.

\paragraph{Empty scene rescan and export.}
To generate complete ground-truth geometry for the background scene, the scanner is re-run after removing all non-structural objects from the scene. This produces a complete empty-scene point cloud and panoramic image, corresponding to what the scanner would observe in the absence of furniture. The combination of the furnished scan, the empty-scene scan, the per-object partial clouds, the object ground-truth meshes, and the per-object and full-scene occlusion grids provides all the supervision signals required to evaluate each stage of the pipeline quantitatively.

% ── 3.4.3 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Procedural Scene Generation}
\label{sec:da_procedural}

To enable large-scale dataset generation without manual scene authoring, the virtual scanner is integrated with a procedural indoor scene generation system implemented in Unity. The generator produces fully furnished single-room scenes automatically, enabling the creation of a large number of diverse layouts for dataset generation.

Room layouts are generated by sampling room dimensions from a configurable range, producing rectangular floor plans of variable size. The floor is discretised into a regular grid of tiles. Walls are placed along the outer boundary, with each wall segment probabilistically assigned as a solid wall, a window, or a doorway based on predefined probabilities; at least one doorway is always included. Ceiling elements are placed at a fixed height above each floor tile. Furniture is placed in two stages: wall-mounted elements such as shelves and cabinets are first positioned along interior walls with a configurable probability, followed by free-standing items placed within the interior. Collision-free placement is enforced by bounding box intersection tests, producing physically plausible layouts.

The procedural system uses configurable asset collections implemented as Unity ScriptableObjects, each defining a visual style for a room through references to prefab models for structural elements and furniture. Switching between asset configurations generates visually diverse scenes while using the same underlying generation algorithm. All random functions are seeded from a single configurable seed, making generation deterministic and reproducible.

After room generation, the virtual scanner is positioned at the centre of the room bounding box and configured to capture the full scene under a chosen device preset. The scan is then executed automatically, with no manual intervention required.

% ── 3.4.4 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{The V-Scan Dataset}
\label{sec:da_vscan}

Using the virtual scanning framework and procedural generation system described above, we introduce the \textit{V-Scan} dataset: a synthetic dataset of partially scanned indoor scenes paired with complete ground-truth geometry at both the scene and object level~\cite{vermandere_synthetic_2025}.

\paragraph{Dataset composition.}
The dataset consists of approximately 100 procedurally generated furnished single-room scenes, each scanned using the Leica P30 preset to simulate high-quality TLS acquisition. Each scene in the dataset includes the following data:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A furnished scene point cloud with colours and surface normals, representing the partial observation from the scanner position.
    \item An empty scene point cloud with colours and normals, representing the ground-truth background geometry without furniture.
    \item Equirectangular panoramic images of both the furnished and empty scenes captured at the scanner position.
    \item A full-scene voxel occlusion grid encoding sensor, inter-object and contact occlusions.
    \item For each non-structural object: a partial point cloud, the object's oriented bounding box (8 corner points in world coordinates), a per-object voxel occlusion grid, and the ground-truth mesh.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Dataset statistics.}
Table~\ref{tab:da_vscan_stats} summarises the key statistics of the V-Scan dataset. Scenes contain between 5 and 20 non-structural objects, reflecting the density range of furnished single rooms. The number of points per furnished scene scan ranges from approximately 2 to 8 million, depending on room size and furniture density. \section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:da_conclusion}

This chapter has described the two data sources used throughout the thesis and characterised their respective properties and limitations. Real-world data, captured across three test sites using static TLS, mobile mapping and XR devices, provides authentic geometric and appearance complexity and enables qualitative validation of pipeline performance. The publicly available datasets — SUN RGB-D, ScanNet, ScanNet++, Matterport3D and Redwood-3DScan — extend the real-world evaluation scope and provide instance-level annotations for training and benchmarking.

All real-world data sources share a fundamental limitation: they cannot provide ground truth for occluded regions, restricting evaluation to the visible surfaces. This limitation motivates the V-Scan synthetic dataset, which provides complete geometric ground truth at both the scene and object level through physically-based ray-casting simulation, procedural scene generation and explicit occlusion annotation. The combination of both data types allows the subsequent chapters to evaluate completion and segmentation methods both qualitatively, on diverse real-world scenes, and quantitatively, against the V-Scan ground truth.
