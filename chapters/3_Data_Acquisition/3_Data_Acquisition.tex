\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:3_intro}

% Paragraph purpose: define the data requirements imposed by the full pipeline
This chapter outlines the data used for the remainder of the thesis. Each stage of the pipeline (Figure \ref{fig:1_Research_Aim}) has specific requirements. At scene level, the input must consist of partially scanned indoor single-room environments with accompanying colour information, covering both residential and industrial contexts. To support the alignment and updating stage (Chapter~\ref{ch:4_Alignment_Updating}), repeated captures of the same environment are required across time and across sensing technologies. To support scene segmentation and object detection (Chapter~\ref{ch:5_Scene_Segmentation}), object-level annotations such as bounding boxes are needed. For scene completion (Chapter~\ref{ch:6_Scene_Completion}), ground-truth geometry of the empty scene is required, while object completion (Chapter~\ref{ch:7_Object_Completion}) additionally requires complete object geometry together with isolated partial scans of individual objects.

% Paragraph purpose: explain why existing datasets are insufficient as a complete solution
To the best of our knowledge, no single existing dataset satisfies all of these requirements simultaneously. Public scene-level datasets such as ScanNet~\cite{dai_scannet_2017} and Matterport3D~\cite{chang_matterport3d_2017} provide realistic indoor scans and useful semantic annotations, but they do not include ground-truth geometry for occluded regions. Synthetic scene datasets can provide controlled annotations and complete geometry, but often fail to reproduce the sampling characteristics, noise behaviour, and occlusion patterns of real scanning devices. At object level, repositories such as ShapeNet~\cite{chang_shapenet_2015} offer large numbers of complete models, but these are CAD assets rather than scanned observations and therefore do not reflect realistic partial acquisition conditions. Conversely, real scanned object datasets such as Redwood~\cite{choi_large_2016} provide realistic reconstructions, but remain limited in scale and diversity.

% Paragraph purpose: introduce the three-source acquisition strategy as a methodological choice
For this reason, this thesis adopts a combined data acquisition strategy based on three complementary sources. First, existing public datasets are used where they provide adequate coverage for specific stages of the pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:3_existing}). Second, real-world multi-temporal and multi-sensory captures are recorded on the university campus to provide heterogeneous observations for alignment, updating, and qualitative evaluation under realistic conditions (Section~\ref{sec:3_realworld}). Third, a synthetic dataset generation framework is developed to produce the V-Scan dataset, which supplies controlled scene-level and object-level ground truth, isolated partial object scans, empty-scene reference data, and multi-sensory as well as multi-temporal variations (Section~\ref{sec:3_synthetic}).

Additionally, Section~\ref{sec:3_comparison} compares all data sources used throughout the thesis, and Section~\ref{sec:3_conclusion} concludes the chapter.

\section{Existing Datasets}
\label{sec:3_existing}

Publicly available datasets remain an essential resource for benchmarking, training and external validation in 3D scene understanding. Because the proposed pipeline operates at both scene level and object level, the datasets are grouped in two categories: Scene-level datasets, typically providing scanned indoor environments, and object-level datasets, consisting of isolated complete object models or scans.

The following sections therefore review the most relevant public datasets according to these two levels of representation.

\subsection{Scene-Level Datasets}
\label{sec:3_scene_datasets}

% Paragraph purpose: introduce why scene datasets matter
Scene-level datasets provide indoor environments containing structural elements, furniture and object clutter. They are particularly relevant for evaluating methods that must reason jointly about room geometry, object placement and partial observations.

Large-scale real-world indoor datasets have played a central role in advancing scene understanding and reconstruction. Early datasets such as \textit{SUN RGB-D}~\cite{song_sun_2015} provided RGB-D images with room layout and object annotations, while \textit{Matterport3D}~\cite{chang_matterport3d_2017}, \textit{ScanNet}~\cite{dai_scannet_2017}, and \textit{Stanford 2D-3D-S}~\cite{armeni_joint_2017} introduced richly annotated indoor environments with registered imagery, depth data, and reconstructed geometry. More recent datasets such as \textit{ARKitScenes}~\cite{baruch_arkitscenes_2022} and \textit{ScanNet++}~\cite{yeshwanth_scannet_2023} further improved scene diversity and geometric quality through mobile LiDAR and refined capture pipelines. These datasets have been highly valuable for semantic segmentation, scene reconstruction, and 3D perception, but they share an important limitation for the purposes of this thesis: they only contain observed geometry. The occluded regions that completion methods are expected to reconstruct are, by definition, absent from the captured data, which prevents direct supervised evaluation of completion quality.

\paragraph{ScanNet++.}

ScanNet++~\cite{yeshwanth_scannet_2023} is one of the primary real-world datasets used for both quantitative and qualitative evaluation of the pipeline. It extends earlier RGB-D indoor datasets such as ScanNet~\cite{dai_scannet_2017} through significantly higher geometric and visual fidelity. The dataset contains more than 1,000 indoor scenes, each captured using a combination of sub-millimetre terrestrial laser scans, registered 33-megapixel DSLR imagery, and commodity RGB-D streams from mobile devices. This multi-modal acquisition strategy provides both highly accurate reference geometry and realistic lower-cost sensor observations within the same environments. (Figure~\ref{fig:3_ScanNet}).

\paragraph{Matterport3D.}

Matterport3D~\cite{chang_matterport3d_2017} is the second primary real-world dataset used for evaluation. It contains 90 building-scale environments comprising approximately 10,800 panoramic viewpoints reconstructed from 194,400 RGB-D images. Captures were obtained using the Matterport Pro camera system and include globally aligned camera poses, textured meshes and semantic annotations (Figure~\ref{fig:3_Matterport3D}).

Each environment is subdivided into multiple rooms, resulting in a large variety of bedrooms, living rooms, kitchens, hallways, offices and commercial interiors. This makes Matterport3D particularly useful for evaluating completion methods under realistic room layouts and varied furnishing styles.

Its widespread adoption in scene understanding and embodied AI research also facilitates comparison with prior work and improves external validity of the reported results.

Together, ScanNet++ and Matterport3D provide two complementary real-world benchmarks: one prioritising geometric fidelity and sensor diversity, the other prioritising spatial diversity and room-scale complexity.

\subsection{Object-Level Datasets}
\label{sec:3_object_datasets}

% Paragraph purpose: explain why object datasets matter
Object-level datasets are required for evaluating isolated object completion methods, where the goal is to reconstruct complete object geometry from partial observations. In this thesis, both synthetic CAD repositories and real scanned object datasets are used to capture complementary aspects of this problem.

Datasets such as the \textit{Redwood Object Scan Dataset}~\cite{choi_large_2016}, \textit{Pix3D}~\cite{sun_pix3d_2018}, \textit{Google Scanned Objects}~\cite{downs_google_2022}, and \textit{OmniObject3D}~\cite{wu_omniobject3d_2023} provide high-quality 3D object models derived from real scanning or image-based reconstruction. These resources are valuable for object reconstruction, novel-view synthesis, and shape modelling, However they remain limited in scale, diversity and annotation quality compared to synthetic repositories like ShapeNet\cite{chang_shapenet_2015}.

\paragraph{ShapeNet.}

ShapeNet~\cite{chang_shapenet_2015} is one of the primary datasets used for quantitative isolated object completion experiments in Chapter~\ref{ch:7_Object_Completion}. It is a large-scale repository of CAD-based 3D object models organised into semantic categories, containing tens of thousands of examples across common furniture and household object classes (Figure~\ref{fig:3_ShapeNet}).

Its principal strength lies in the availability of clean and complete geometry, making it well suited for controlled benchmarking and for training data-hungry completion networks. Because models are complete and consistently structured, ShapeNet enables reliable geometric error measurement between predicted and reference shapes.

Unlike ShapeNet, Redwood reflects the imperfections of real-world acquisition, including sensor noise, incomplete observations and reconstruction artefacts. This makes it particularly valuable for assessing whether methods trained or benchmarked on synthetic CAD models remain effective on realistic scanned objects.

\subsection{Summary}

% Paragraph purpose: summarise dataset choices and motivate synthetic V-Scan
The public datasets used in this thesis were selected according to the specific needs of each pipeline stage rather than through a one-size-fits-all benchmark strategy. ScanNet++ and Matterport3D are used as the principal real-scene datasets for quantitative evaluation of the object detection and scene segmentation stages, and qualitative evaluation of the full pipeline. ShapeNet and Redwood are used for quantitative evaluation of the most complex step: isolated object completion.

Despite their individual strengths, none of these datasets simultaneously provide scene-level partial scans, complete hidden geometry, object-level isolated partial observations, multi-temporal variation and multi-sensory captures. This remaining gap motivates the need for additional real-world captures and the creation of a synthetic dataset.

\section{Real-World Data Capture}
\label{sec:3_realworld}

\subsection{Introduction}

The first set of manually scanned data used in this thesis consists of real-world captures performed at the KU~Leuven Technology Campus in Ghent, Belgium. These captures serve two primary purposes: providing multi-temporal and multi-sensory data for evaluating the alignment and updating pipeline (Chapter~\ref{ch:4_Alignment_Updating}), and providing real indoor scenes with diverse object configurations for qualitative evaluation of the detection and completion stages. The captures cover a range of industrial laboratory spaces and classroom-style environments, using a combination of high-end terrestrial laser scanners and consumer-grade \gls{XR} devices.

\paragraph{Terrestrial Laser Scanners.}
Static \gls{TLS} systems such as the Leica ScanStation P30 \cite{noauthor_leica_nodate} and BLK360 \cite{noauthor_leica_nodate-1} provide the highest measurement accuracy, typically achieving single-point accuracies below 5\,mm at ranges up to 50\,m~\cite{bassier_standalone_2016}. These systems capture structured point grids of 10 to 40 million points per setup, along with panoramic imagery. However, the static nature of the measurement introduces significant occlusions at ground level and behind objects, as only surfaces visible from the scanner position are recorded. Consecutive setups must be registered, typically through a combination of target-based and cloud-to-cloud methods, with sub-millimetre error propagation achievable across setups~\cite{geyter_point_2022}.

\paragraph{Indoor Mobile Mapping Systems.}
Indoor mobile mapping systems (\gls{iMMS}) such as the NavVis VLX \cite{noauthor_navvis_nodate} combine LiDAR sensors with camera-based \gls{SLAM} algorithms to achieve continuous data acquisition while the operator moves through the environment. These systems produce unordered point clouds with uneven point distributions, but their continuous trajectory results in significantly better spatial coverage than static scanners, particularly in cluttered environments. Global accuracies of LOA20 ($2\sigma \leq 0.05$\,m) to LOA30 ($2\sigma \leq 0.015$\,m) are achievable when supported by control measurements~\cite{geyter_point_2022, vermandere_two-step_2022}. Data acquisition is typically two to three times faster than with static TLS.

\paragraph{XR Devices.}
Extended Reality devices such as the Microsoft HoloLens~2 \cite{hubner_evaluation_2020} represent the lower end of the capture spectrum. These head-mounted devices are equipped with a 1\,MP \gls{ToF} sensor and four grey-scale tracking cameras, producing real-time polygonal meshes at a default spatial resolution of approximately 0.08\,m$^3$~\cite{geyter_point_2022,hubner_evaluation_2020}. While their accuracy is limited, with single-point accuracies of approximately 1\,cm/m and a maximum useful range of 3--4\,m, they offer unparalleled mobility and can capture regions inaccessible to larger systems. The accompanying RGB camera provides localised imagery that can be used for 2D-based alignment methods.

\paragraph{Smartphone LiDAR.}
Recent smartphones equipped with LiDAR sensors, such as iPhone models with the Apple LiDAR \cite{abdel-majeed_indoor_2024} Scanner, provide another commodity-level capture modality. These devices produce dense point clouds at close range with moderate accuracy, and their widespread availability makes them relevant for incremental updating scenarios where dedicated scanning equipment is not practical.


\subsection{Capture Methodology}
\label{sec:3_capture_method}

Each sensing technology was deployed according to its intended use case \cite{geyter_point_2022}. For TLS captures, one or two scanner setups were placed per room, positioning the scanner to maximise coverage of the structural elements while accepting the inherent ground-level occlusions caused by furniture and equipment. For iMMS captures, the operator walked through the environment following a natural trajectory, ensuring loop closures where possible to minimise drift accumulation. For XR device captures, the operator walked around the room attempting to capture all accessible surfaces, including regions underneath and behind furniture that are typically harder to reach with larger scanners.

This deliberate combination of sensors and deployment strategies produces complementary datasets: TLS data provides high-accuracy structural geometry with systematic occlusions, iMMS data provides high-coverage reconstructions with moderate accuracy, and XR data provides fast, accessible captures with reduced accuracy but improved coverage in cluttered areas.

\subsection{Captured Scenes}
\label{sec:3_captured_scenes}

% Paragraph purpose: introduce the set of captured environments
Three categories of indoor environments were captured across the KU~Leuven campus in Ghent and at a residential site in Bruges (Figure~\ref{fig:3_captured_scenes}). These comprise industrial laboratory rooms used for soil, asphalt, and building physics research, a prefab living lab constructed on the campus, and a four-storey residential renovation in Bruges. Each environment was documented at multiple time points and with multiple sensors, producing the multi-temporal and multi-sensory data required for the alignment experiments in Chapter~\ref{ch:4_Alignment_Updating}. Between capture epochs, the environments were in active use or under active construction, resulting in natural variations in object placement, surface treatment, and structural configuration.

% Paragraph purpose: describe the laboratory rooms
The laboratory rooms are single-storey industrial spaces with footprints on the order of 10\,m~$\times$~30\,m, containing working desks, test equipment, machinery, and bulk materials. Between captures taken several months apart, all movable objects were displaced in the course of ongoing research activity. These rooms serve as the primary test case for multi-temporal alignment under layout change in an otherwise stable structural envelope.

% Paragraph purpose: describe the living lab
The living lab is a three-storey prefab residential building erected on the campus as a dedicated research facility. It was captured repeatedly throughout its construction, from early stake-out through to the installation of mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) systems. This site provides test data in which both the geometry and the surface appearance of the scene change drastically between epochs, as structural elements are added and interior finishes are applied.

% Paragraph purpose: describe the Bruges renovation
The Bruges renovation is a four-storey residential building including a full basement and attic, captured during an interior refurbishment. Here the structural envelope is largely fixed, but the interior is cluttered with construction materials, tools, and temporary storage that change position between visits. This site complements the living lab by providing a residential-scale renovation environment rather than a new-build construction site.

In summary, the real-world dataset provides a heterogeneous collection of indoor scenes captured with complementary sensing technologies spanning high-accuracy terrestrial scanners to highly mobile consumer devices. \section{Synthetic Data Creation}
\label{sec:3_synthetic}

\subsection{Introduction}

While the real-world captures described in the previous section provide heterogeneous multi-sensory data for alignment evaluation, they cannot provide the ground-truth geometry required for quantitative evaluation of scene and object completion. The surfaces that completion methods are expected to reconstruct are precisely those that remain unobserved in real scans, and therefore cannot be measured directly. For this reason, quantitative evaluation requires a synthetic data generation strategy in which the complete geometry of the scene is known, while the observed input remains partial and sensor-dependent.

To address this requirement, this thesis introduces a synthetic data creation framework that combines procedurally generated indoor scenes with a virtualised scanning process. The methodological aim is not merely to create artificial scenes, but to generate scan data that reproduces the structural characteristics of real acquisition: partial visibility, occlusion patterns, varying point densities, sensor noise, and differences between capture modalities. The resulting framework enables the construction of paired datasets in which each partial scan is accompanied by complete ground-truth geometry at both scene and object level. This is essential for evaluating detection, completion, and dynamification in a controlled and reproducible manner.

The framework is built around two complementary components. First, a virtual scanning methodology simulates how different sensing technologies observe a scene from a particular viewpoint, thereby producing realistic partial measurements rather than idealised full geometry. Second, a procedural scene generation methodology produces diverse indoor layouts with controllable object arrangements, making it possible to vary room structure, furniture composition, and temporal changes across repeated captures. Together, these components form the basis of the V-Scan dataset introduced in Section~\ref{sec:3_vscan}.

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:3_synthetic_related}

Synthetic data generation for 3D vision builds on several strands of prior work, including real-world indoor scan datasets, synthetic scene and object repositories, virtual sensing frameworks, and procedural scene generation methods.

Scene-level datasets such as \textit{SUNCG}~\cite{song_semantic_2016}, \textit{SceneNet RGB-D}~\cite{mccormac_scenenet_nodate}, \textit{InteriorNet}~\cite{li_interiornet_2018}, and \textit{Replica}~\cite{straub_replica_2019} offer procedurally generated or reconstructed indoor environments with photorealistic rendering and ground-truth geometry. At the object level, repositories such as \textit{ShapeNet}~\cite{chang_shapenet_2015}, \textit{ModelNet}~\cite{wu_3d_2015}, and \textit{Amazon Berkeley Objects}~\cite{collins_abo_2022} provide extensive collections of CAD models and scanned objects for classification, reconstruction, and shape analysis. While these datasets provide clean geometry, they typically represent complete scenes or complete objects directly, rather than simulating the partial and sensor-dependent observations from which reconstruction pipelines must operate.

To bridge this gap, several works have explored virtual scanning and LiDAR simulation. Early approaches such as the laser scanner simulator of Blume~et~al.~\cite{blume_simulating_2006} focused on efficient range simulation for robotics applications. More recent systems such as the virtual scanner of Popovas~et~al.~\cite{popovas_virtual_2021} and \textit{HELIOS++}~\cite{winiwarter_virtual_2022} simulate viewpoint-dependent sensing and measurement behaviour with high realism. These frameworks demonstrate the importance of modelling the acquisition process itself rather than relying solely on idealised geometry. However, they are generally designed for surveying, robotics, or training applications, and do not explicitly support the generation of object-level ground truth, scene-object decomposition, or completion-oriented supervision within furnished indoor environments.

Another relevant line of work concerns procedural scene generation. Rather than manually constructing environments one by one, procedural and generative methods make it possible to produce large numbers of diverse indoor scenes in a scalable and reproducible manner. \textit{Infinigen Indoors}~\cite{raistrick_infinigen_nodate} generates photorealistic indoor scenes using a constraint-based procedural framework with large asset libraries. \textit{RoomPilot}~\cite{chen_roompilot_2025} translates textual descriptions or floor plans into structured indoor layouts through a domain-specific scene representation, while \textit{DiffuScene}~\cite{tang_diffuscene_2024} uses diffusion-based generation to synthesise plausible indoor object arrangements. These approaches highlight the increasing realism and controllability of synthetic indoor scene generation, but they are not primarily designed to create benchmark data for viewpoint-dependent scanning, object isolation, and completion evaluation.

The synthetic framework proposed in this thesis builds on these strands of work while addressing a more specific methodological need: the creation of furnished indoor scenes that can be virtually scanned under different sensing conditions, while preserving complete ground truth at both scene and object level. In contrast to existing indoor scan datasets, it provides access to the geometry hidden by occlusion. In contrast to generic synthetic scene repositories, it explicitly models the sensing process that gives rise to partial point clouds. And in contrast to existing virtual scanning frameworks, it is designed not only to simulate acquisition, but also to support object isolation, empty-scene reference generation, and controlled variation in sensor type, viewpoint, and scene configuration. These properties are essential for constructing the V-Scan dataset used throughout the remainder of this thesis.

\subsection{Virtual Scanner}
\label{sec:3_virtual_scanner}

The first methodological component of the framework is a virtual scanner that emulates the acquisition process of real sensing devices within a synthetic 3D environment. Its purpose is to transform complete virtual geometry into partial observations that exhibit the same types of incompleteness encountered in practice. In other words, rather than using synthetic scenes directly as learning or evaluation inputs, the framework passes them through a simulated measurement process so that the resulting data resembles actual scan output.



The scanner is designed around the observation that different sensing technologies vary not only in accuracy, but also in sampling density, spatial coverage, field of view, and noise behaviour. These factors strongly influence downstream tasks such as alignment, detection, and completion. The virtual scanner therefore models each device through a set of acquisition parameters controlling angular sampling, maximum range, vertical field of view, and measurement uncertainty. By varying these parameters, the framework reproduces the behaviour of high-end terrestrial laser scanners, mobile mapping systems, and commodity-level sensors such as smartphone LiDAR or \gls{XR} devices.

From a methodological perspective, the key principle is that partiality should emerge naturally from visibility constraints rather than being imposed artificially. The scan is therefore generated from a fixed viewpoint by evaluating which surfaces are visible along a spherical sampling pattern. This produces realistic self-occlusions, blind zones, and distance-dependent sparsity. Measurement uncertainty is then introduced through a noise model that combines a range-independent component, representing intrinsic sensor noise, with a range-dependent component, reflecting the degradation of accuracy with increasing distance. In this way, the synthetic point cloud captures not only the geometry that is observed, but also the imperfections with which it is observed.

In addition to geometry, the scanner also produces appearance information through a co-located panoramic image. This mirrors the acquisition principle of real terrestrial scanning systems, where colour is often derived from panoramic imagery rather than measured directly by the range sensor. The combination of point cloud and panoramic image is particularly important for this thesis, as later stages rely on both geometric and visual cues.

A further methodological requirement is the ability to evaluate object-level completion separately from scene-level completion. For this reason, the scanning process is coupled to object annotations within the synthetic environment. This makes it possible to extract, for each object, both the partial observation produced by the scan and the corresponding complete ground-truth geometry. The same scene can thus be analysed at multiple levels: as a furnished partial scan, as an empty structural scene, and as a collection of partially observed objects with known complete shapes.

\subsection{Procedural Room Layout Generator}
\label{sec:3_room_generator}

The second methodological component is a procedural room layout generator that creates furnished indoor environments with controlled variability. Its role is to ensure that the synthetic dataset does not overfit to a small number of manually modelled scenes, but instead spans a broader range of room sizes, object constellations, and occlusion patterns.

The room generation strategy follows a modular logic. Each scene is composed of a structural envelope---floor, walls, ceiling, and openings---combined with an interior arrangement of furniture and equipment. This separation between structure and contents is deliberate. It reflects the conceptual distinction used throughout the thesis between background scene elements and foreground objects, and it enables systematic variation of one while holding the other constant. In particular, furniture can be regenerated within the same room shell to create multi-temporal variants of the same environment, thereby mimicking real spaces whose structure remains stable while their contents change over time.

Furniture placement is designed to balance diversity with plausibility. Objects are not inserted arbitrarily, but according to simple spatial rules that distinguish wall-related elements from free-standing ones and prevent impossible overlaps. This yields scenes that remain procedurally diverse while preserving the basic spatial coherence of indoor environments. The resulting layouts contain both isolated and densely clustered objects, which is important because completion quality depends strongly on the degree and type of occlusion present in the scene.

A further methodological requirement is reproducibility. All generation decisions are therefore controlled by a random seed, allowing the exact same scene to be recreated when needed. This is particularly useful for ablation studies and controlled experiments, since it allows individual factors such as scanner type, scanner position, or object arrangement to be varied independently without introducing uncontrolled changes elsewhere in the scene.

\subsection{Scan Management and Dataset Generation}
\label{sec:3_scan_management}

The synthetic framework is ultimately intended as a dataset-generation methodology rather than a collection of isolated tools. For that reason, scene generation and virtual scanning are integrated into a structured pipeline that produces complete benchmark entries in a consistent and repeatable way.

For each synthetic room, the methodology begins by generating a furnished scene and identifying the objects of interest within it. A primary scan is then simulated to produce the partial observation of the furnished environment. From this same scene state, object-level partial observations and corresponding complete object geometries are derived. Next, the foreground objects are removed and the scene is scanned again, producing a complete structural reference of the empty room. This dual acquisition strategy is essential: it allows scene completion methods to be evaluated against an object-free structural ground truth, while object completion methods can be evaluated against their own isolated complete meshes.

To support controlled experimentation, additional scan variants are generated by changing one or more acquisition variables while keeping the underlying scene either fixed or partially modified. These variations include changes in sensor type, scanner position, and furniture configuration. Methodologically, this allows the dataset to serve not just as a benchmark for static reconstruction, but also as a testbed for robustness: one can study how methods behave under different sensing qualities, different viewpoints, or temporal changes in object placement.

This design makes the synthetic dataset particularly suitable for the broader goals of the thesis. The same generated environment can be used to evaluate alignment under cross-sensor variation, detection under cluttered configurations, scene completion under structural occlusion, and object completion under partial visibility. In this sense, the framework functions as a unifying experimental substrate across multiple chapters rather than as a standalone data-generation exercise.

\subsection{The V-Scan Dataset}
\label{sec:3_vscan}

The V-Scan dataset is the concrete outcome of the synthetic methodology described above and serves as the main benchmark for the quantitative evaluation of scene and object completion in this thesis. Its design is guided by a specific methodological objective: to provide, within a single dataset, the forms of ground truth and controlled variation that are unavailable in real-world capture campaigns.

Unlike conventional synthetic datasets that provide only complete scene models or only isolated object shapes, V-Scan represents each scene simultaneously as: a furnished partial scan, an empty structural reference, and a set of object-level partial observations paired with complete object meshes. This multi-level representation is critical for the evaluation framework adopted in this thesis, since the different pipeline stages operate on different targets and require different forms of supervision and benchmarking.

The dataset also incorporates variation across scanner types and scene states. As a result, it does not represent a single idealised scan per room, but a family of related observations generated under controlled changes in sensing conditions and object layout. This makes it possible to assess not only absolute reconstruction quality, but also sensitivity to data quality, viewpoint, and temporal modification. The dataset therefore supports a broader methodological agenda: evaluating whether the proposed pipeline remains effective across the kinds of variation that real digital-twin workflows inevitably encounter.

For each scene entry, the dataset provides the partial scene scan, the associated image data, the structural ground truth of the empty room, object-level partial scans, complete object meshes, and the annotations required for detection and localisation. Taken together, these outputs enable quantitative comparison at scene level, object level, and detection level within a single unified benchmark. In doing so, V-Scan provides the experimental foundation required to evaluate the central claim of this thesis: that incomplete reality-capture data can be transformed into structured, geometrically complete, and manipulable scene representations.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:3_conclusion}

This chapter presented the data acquisition strategy adopted in this thesis, addressing the diverse requirements of the proposed pipeline through a combination of three complementary data sources. Publicly available datasets provide large-scale, diverse real-world scenes for training, qualitative evaluation, and generalisation assessment. Real-world multi-temporal and multi-sensory captures from the university campus provide heterogeneous data for evaluating the alignment and updating pipeline. The novel V-Scan synthetic dataset, generated through a virtual scanning and procedural room generation framework, provides the comprehensive ground-truth data required for quantitative evaluation of scene and object completion methods.

The virtual scanning framework introduced in this chapter simulates the acquisition characteristics of seven different scanner types, ranging from high-end terrestrial laser scanners to consumer-grade XR devices, by configuring scan density, range, field of view, and noise parameters. The procedural room generator produces diverse furnished indoor environments with controllable complexity. Together, these components enable the automated generation of large-scale datasets with complete ground-truth geometry at both the scene and object level, multi-temporal variations through furniture regeneration, and multi-sensory variations through scanner type switching.

The V-Scan dataset, containing 200 scene folders with 3,100 total objects, serves as the primary benchmark for the quantitative evaluation conducted in the subsequent chapters. Its combination of scene-level and object-level ground truth, multi-temporal captures, and multi-sensory variations addresses the evaluation gap identified in Chapter~\ref{ch:introduction}, where the absence of ground truth for occluded regions was identified as a fundamental barrier to the quantitative assessment of completion methods.
